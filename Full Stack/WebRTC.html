WebRTC Guide

A flow-first guide to building real-time video call applications. This guide teaches the sequence of events and the mental model behind WebRTC — not isolated API definitions. It covers multi-user rooms, starting with no active tracks, and toggling audio, video, and screen share mid-call.

💡

Most tutorials only cover one-to-one calls where both users start with camera and mic already on. Real products are more complex: rooms have N users, users join with everything off, and tracks are toggled on and off at any point. This guide addresses all three realities.

What WebRTC Is

WebRTC (Web Real-Time Communication) is a browser API that allows two browsers to exchange audio, video, and data directly with each other — without routing media through a server. Once two browsers establish a WebRTC connection, all media travels on a direct peer-to-peer channel. The server is only needed during the initial handshake to help two browsers find and describe each other.

Simple version: Two people want to talk privately. They need a mutual friend (your server) to exchange contact details exactly once. After that, they communicate directly. The mutual friend is out of the picture.

Foundations — The Complete Picture

Before touching any API, you need a mental model of what WebRTC is actually trying to do. The API makes no sense without it. This section explains the problem from scratch, introduces every piece of terminology used throughout this guide, and shows you the complete picture of how a WebRTC connection comes to life.

Why Peer-to-Peer Is Hard

The normal model for internet communication is client-server. You open YouTube, your browser connects to YouTube's server, and the server sends you a video. Easy. The server has a public address that anyone can reach. Your browser just dials it.

Peer-to-peer flips this. Instead of connecting to a well-known server, two browsers — both sitting inside ordinary homes, offices, or mobile networks — need to connect directly to each other. This is surprisingly difficult for one reason: neither browser has a public address that the other can directly dial.

⚠

Your laptop does not have a public IP address. It has a local IP like 192.168.1.42 — meaningful only inside your home router. The outside world cannot reach you at that address. Neither can another browser trying to connect to you. This is called being behind a NAT (Network Address Translator), and almost every device on the internet is behind one.

So two browsers need to find paths to each other through a maze of routers, NATs, and firewalls — paths that neither browser knows about ahead of time. WebRTC's job is to discover those paths automatically and pick the best one.

The Two Fundamental Problems WebRTC Solves

Every concept in WebRTC maps back to one of two problems. Keep these in mind as you read the rest of this guide — they explain why every step exists.

Problem 1 — "How do I reach you?"

Two browsers need to discover a working network path between them through NATs and firewalls. This is solved by ICE — the browser automatically tries many possible routes and picks the first one that works.

Problem 2 — "What format do you speak?"

Two browsers may support different audio codecs, video resolutions, or compression formats. They need to agree on a common format before sending any media. This is solved by SDP — each side describes its capabilities and they negotiate a match.

Everything else in WebRTC — the offer, the answer, STUN, TURN, renegotiation — is infrastructure built to solve these two problems. Once you see that, the API stops being a collection of mysterious calls and starts being a logical sequence of steps.

A WebRTC connection always goes through exactly three phases in order. Phase 1 requires your server. Phases 2 and 3 happen entirely between the two browsers.

Terminology Glossary

These are all the terms used throughout this guide. Read through them once now — each one will make sense in the context of the diagram above.

TermWhat it isAnalogy
Peer Any participant in the connection. In a two-person call, you are one peer, the other person is the other peer. In a room of five, everyone is a peer. A person on a phone call
Peer-to-peer (P2P) Communication that travels directly between two browsers, with no server in the path once the connection is established. Media bytes go: your browser → their browser, nothing in between. Two people talking directly rather than through an operator
Signalling The process of exchanging the setup information (SDP and ICE candidates) needed to establish the direct connection. Signalling always goes through your server. The word "signalling" means "coordination before the real communication begins." Exchanging phone numbers before calling
Signalling server Your server (typically a WebSocket server) that relays SDP and ICE messages between peers during Phase 1. It does not process or inspect the content — it just forwards. Once Phase 2 completes, it is not involved in the call at all. A mutual friend who passes notes between two people who don't know each other yet
SDP (Session Description Protocol) A text document that describes one browser's media capabilities: which audio codecs it supports, which video resolutions, how many tracks it wants to send, and in which direction. Two peers exchange SDPs to find common ground. You never write SDP manually — the browser generates it. A menu of what you can offer, sent to a restaurant so they can match what they serve
Offer The first SDP document in the negotiation, created by the Caller using createOffer(). It says: "Here are all the formats I can send and receive. What works for you?" Sending your resume first
Answer The SDP document created by the Callee in response to an Offer, using createAnswer(). It says: "From everything you offered, here is what I accept and here is what I can send back." The response letter after reviewing a resume
ICE (Interactive Connectivity Establishment) The system that discovers network paths between two peers. After setLocalDescription() is called, the browser automatically collects a list of addresses at which it might be reachable — local network addresses, public addresses found via STUN, and relay addresses from TURN. These are the ICE candidates. Trying every possible door in a building to find one that is unlocked
ICE Candidate A single possible network address through which this browser might be reachable. A browser typically generates 3–10 candidates per connection: local IP, public IP, and relay. Both peers exchange all their candidates and try each combination until one pair works. One possible phone number to try reaching someone
STUN server A tiny public server that tells your browser its own public IP address — the address that the outside world sees you as. Your browser asks STUN: "What is my public IP?" STUN replies, and that public IP becomes one of your ICE candidates. Google provides a free STUN server at stun.l.google.com:19302. A mirror that shows you what you look like from outside your house
TURN server A fallback relay server used when a direct P2P path cannot be established (e.g. both peers are behind strict firewalls). Media is routed through the TURN server instead of directly. This is the only case where media touches a server after Phase 1. TURN is needed in roughly 10–15% of connections in production. A postal service when direct delivery is blocked — the letter still gets there, just via a carrier
Codec A compression format for audio or video. Common audio codecs: Opus, G.711. Common video codecs: VP8, VP9, H.264. Two peers must agree on a codec that both support before sending media. SDP negotiation handles this automatically. A language — both sides need to speak the same one
Caller / Callee For every pair of peers, one must initiate. The Caller creates and sends the Offer. The Callee receives it and sends the Answer. In a room-based app, the joining user is always the Caller for every new connection they form. Caller = the one who dials. Callee = the one who picks up.
Renegotiation A new offer-answer cycle that happens on an already connected pair when something changes — a track is added, a track is removed, or a codec needs updating. The connection stays open; only the SDP is updated to reflect the new state. Amending a contract without ending the working relationship

How the Path Discovery Actually Works

This is the part most guides skip, but it demystifies everything that follows. When a browser generates ICE candidates, it is building a list of every possible address at which it might be reachable. It does this by checking three sources.

  HOW A BROWSER DISCOVERS ITS ICE CANDIDATES

  Your Browser
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Source 1: Local network interfaces
  └─> 192.168.1.42:12345           ← your laptop's local IP (only works on same WiFi)
  └─> [::1]:12345                  ← IPv6 loopback (usually filtered out)

  Source 2: STUN server (asks "what's my public IP?")
  └─> Browser ──────────────────────────> stun.l.google.com:19302
  └─> STUN replies: "You appear as 203.0.113.78:54321"
  └─> 203.0.113.78:54321           ← your public IP (works across the internet)

  Source 3: TURN server (relay fallback)
  └─> Browser connects to your TURN server
  └─> TURN assigns a relay address: 198.51.100.10:3478
  └─> 198.51.100.10:3478           ← relay (works when direct paths fail)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  All three get sent to the remote peer as ICE candidates.
  The remote peer tries connecting to each one.
  The first pair that succeeds on both sides becomes the connection.

What Your Code Actually Does (The One-Sentence Summary)

Your code's entire job in WebRTC is to be the messenger between the two browsers during Phase 1. The browser generates the SDP and ICE automatically. You just pick them up and pass them to the other side via your server. Once both sides have exchanged enough information, the browser handles Phase 2 and Phase 3 entirely on its own.

✓

A useful way to read this guide: every function you write is either (a) creating a message the browser generated, (b) delivering a message to the other peer, or (c) handling a message that arrived from the other peer. The browser does the hard network work. You write the postal service.

RTCPeerConnection — Properties & Methods

RTCPeerConnection is the central object in WebRTC. One instance manages the complete lifecycle of a peer-to-peer connection with a single remote user — SDP negotiation, ICE transport, media senders, and receivers. Every other concept in this guide flows through it.

These read-only properties reflect the current phase of the connection and are the single source of truth for what the PC is doing at any moment. Read them before calling any method that depends on state.

SDP and ICE

These two concepts represent the "contact detail exchange" that happens before a direct connection forms. You do not write them manually — the browser generates them. Your job is only to pass them to the other peer via your server at the right moment.

SDP — Session Description Protocol

A text blob describing capabilities: which codecs are supported, how many tracks will be sent, and in which direction. The Caller generates an Offer SDP. The Callee generates an Answer SDP. Together they negotiate a common format.

ICE — Interactive Connectivity Establishment

A list of network addresses ("candidates") through which this browser can be reached. Generated automatically after setLocalDescription() is called. Includes local IPs, public IPs via STUN, and relay addresses via TURN. Both peers exchange candidates until a working path is found.

⚠

A STUN server is needed for connections across the internet. Google's public server stun:stun.l.google.com:19302 works for development. For production, also configure a TURN server — STUN alone fails in some corporate networks and mobile environments.

The Signalling Server

WebRTC does not define how two peers find each other or exchange SDP and ICE. That is left to you. The mechanism used to carry those messages is called the signalling layer — typically a WebSocket server.

The server receives messages from one peer and forwards them to another. It never inspects or modifies media. Once the WebRTC connection is established, the signalling server is no longer involved in the call at all.

  Phase 1: SIGNALLING (server is active)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Browser A  ---[SDP Offer]----->  Server  ---[SDP Offer]----->  Browser B
  Browser A  <--[SDP Answer]----  Server  <--[SDP Answer]------  Browser B
  Browser A  ---[ICE cands]---->  Server  ---[ICE cands]----->   Browser B
  Browser A  <--[ICE cands]----   Server  <--[ICE cands]------   Browser B

  Phase 2: CONNECTION ESTABLISHED (server steps out)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Browser A  <=================== direct P2P channel ==================>  Browser B

  Phase 3: MEDIA FLOWS (server not involved)
  ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Browser A  <====== audio tracks / video tracks / data =============>  Browser B

The minimum socket events your server must relay for WebRTC to function: offer, answer, and ice-candidate. The server just receives and forwards each — it does not need to understand the content.

Caller and Callee

For every WebRTC connection between two peers, one must go first. The one who initiates is the Caller. The one who responds is the Callee. One sends the Offer. The other sends the Answer. This asymmetry must be deliberate — if both sides send offers simultaneously, you get a "glare" condition and the connection fails.

💡

The most reliable convention for a room-based app: the joining user is always the Caller. When user C joins a room where A and B exist, C sends offers to both A and B. A and B only answer — they never send their own offers back to C. This guarantees exactly one offer per pair, no glare.

CALLER — the joining user

Receives the list of users already in the room. Creates one RTCPeerConnection per existing user and sends an Offer to each. Awaits an Answer from each.

CALLEE — each existing user

Receives an offer socket event from the newly joined user. Creates an RTCPeerConnection for that connection, sets the remote description, and sends an Answer back.

Step 1 — Join and Announce

When a user arrives at the room, the first action is to notify the server of their presence. The server responds with two distinct events.

1
Client emits join-room

Send a socket event to the server with the room identifier and user identity.

2
Server replies: get-others (to this user only)

The server sends the list of users already in the room back to the socket that just joined. If the room was empty, the list is empty and no connections need to be formed yet.

3
Server broadcasts: user-joined (to all existing users)

The server notifies every other user in the room that someone arrived. This fires on every existing user's socket — not on the joiner themselves.

  User C joins. A and B are already in the room.

  C's socket                  Server               A's socket      B's socket
  ──────────                  ──────               ──────────      ──────────
  emit("join-room") ────────> register C
                              emit("get-others") ──> [A,B] ──> C only
                              emit("user-joined") ─────────────────────────> A
                              emit("user-joined") ──────────────────────────────> B

  C receives [A, B]          A receives user-joined=C      B receives user-joined=C
  C is the CALLER            A is the CALLEE               B is the CALLEE

Step 2 — Create the RTCPeerConnection

Before any offer or answer is exchanged, an RTCPeerConnection object must be created and its event listeners attached. This function is called by both sides — the Caller calls it before sending an offer; the Callee calls it upon receiving the user-joined event.

Four events fire automatically on every RTCPeerConnection. You never call them manually.

EventFires whenYour responsibility
onicecandidateAUTO Browser finds a network path after setLocalDescriptionSend the candidate to the remote peer via socket
ontrackAUTO Remote peer's media track arrives over the connectionAttach the stream to the remote user's video element
oniceconnectionstatechangeAUTO State changes: checking → connected → disconnected → failedLog state, call restartIce() on failure
onnegotiationneededAUTO A track was added or removed and SDP needs updatingRun a new offer-answer cycle with the remote peer
createPCWith(remoteUser)JavaScript
function createPCWith(remoteUser) {
    const pc = new RTCPeerConnection({
        iceServers: [{ urls: "stun:stun.l.google.com:19302" }]
    });
    pcMap.set(remoteUser.socketId, pc);

    // Fires automatically: send each discovered network path to the remote peer.
    pc.onicecandidate = (e) => {
        if (e.candidate)
            socket.emit("ice-candidate", { candidate: e.candidate, targetSocketId: remoteUser.socketId });
    };

    // Fires automatically: remote peer's media has arrived — show it.
    pc.ontrack = (e) => {
        const vid = document.getElementById(`video-${remoteUser.socketId}`);
        if (vid && e.streams[0]) vid.srcObject = e.streams[0];
    };

    // Fires automatically: restart if the path fails.
    pc.oniceconnectionstatechange = () => {
        if (pc.iceConnectionState === "failed") pc.restartIce();
    };

    // Fires automatically: a track was added/removed — run a new offer-answer cycle.
    pc.onnegotiationneeded = async () => {
        if (pc.signalingState !== "stable") return; // guard against concurrent triggers
        await sendOfferTo(remoteUser, pc);
    };

    return pc;
}

Step 3 — The Caller Sends an Offer

After creating the PC, the Caller (the joining user) generates an Offer SDP and transmits it. This is a three-step sequence: create, commit locally, transmit.

  CALLER side

  pc.createOffer()
    └─> browser generates an SDP blob describing what you can send/receive

  pc.setLocalDescription(offer)
    └─> you commit to this SDP on your side
    └─> browser IMMEDIATELY starts generating ICE candidates
    └─> pc.onicecandidate begins firing (asynchronously, multiple times)

  socket.emit("offer", { offer, targetSocketId })
    └─> server receives and forwards to the target socket
sendOfferTo(remoteUser, pc) + get-others handlerJavaScript
async function sendOfferTo(remoteUser, pc) {
    const offer = await pc.createOffer();
    await pc.setLocalDescription(offer);
    // ICE candidate generation starts automatically in the background now.
    socket.emit("offer", { offer, targetSocketId: remoteUser.socketId });
}

// The joining user (Caller) receives the list of existing users and offers to each.
socket.on("get-others", (others) => {
    for (const user of others) {
        userMap.set(user.socketId, user);
        addUserToUI(user);
        const pc = createPCWith(user);
        sendOfferTo(user, pc); // Caller sends offer to each existing user
    }
});
⚠

When an existing user receives user-joined, they should not send an offer. They create the PC and wait. The joining user is the Caller — they send the offer, existing users answer it. If both sides send offers, you get a glare condition and connection fails.

user-joined handler — existing users (Callees)JavaScript
// Existing users are told someone joined. They prepare a PC but do NOT send an offer.
socket.on("user-joined", (remoteUser) => {
    userMap.set(remoteUser.socketId, remoteUser);
    addUserToUI(remoteUser);
    createPCWith(remoteUser); // Create and store. No offer. Waits for one from joiner.
});

Step 4 — The Callee Answers

The existing user receives the offer via socket, sets it as remote description, generates an Answer SDP, commits to it locally, and sends it back.

  CALLEE side — socket.on("offer") fires

  pc.setRemoteDescription(offer)
    └─> Callee now knows Caller's capabilities

  pc.createAnswer()
    └─> browser generates Answer SDP matching what both sides support

  pc.setLocalDescription(answer)
    └─> Callee commits to the answer
    └─> Callee's ICE candidates start generating

  socket.emit("answer", { answer, targetSocketId })
    └─> server forwards it back to the Caller
offer and answer handlersJavaScript
// Existing user (Callee) receives the offer, answers it.
socket.on("offer", async ({ offer, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId); // PC was already created in user-joined
    await pc.setRemoteDescription(new RTCSessionDescription(offer));
    const answer = await pc.createAnswer();
    await pc.setLocalDescription(answer);
    socket.emit("answer", { answer, targetSocketId: senderSocketId });
});

// Joining user (Caller) receives the answer and completes its own SDP setup.
socket.on("answer", async ({ answer, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);
    if (pc) await pc.setRemoteDescription(new RTCSessionDescription(answer));
});

Step 5 — ICE Candidate Exchange

After each side calls setLocalDescription, the browser asynchronously discovers multiple network paths and fires onicecandidate for each one. These are sent to the remote peer, who adds them via addIceCandidate(). The browser tries all candidates and uses the best working path.

!

Race condition: ICE candidates can arrive at the remote peer via socket before setRemoteDescription() has been called on their PC. Calling addIceCandidate() before setting the remote description throws an error. Wrap it in try-catch at minimum. For production, maintain a pending candidates queue per socketId and flush it after setRemoteDescription completes.

socket.on("ice-candidate")JavaScript
socket.on("ice-candidate", async ({ candidate, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);
    if (pc && candidate) {
        try {
            await pc.addIceCandidate(new RTCIceCandidate(candidate));
        } catch (e) {
            console.warn("ICE candidate error (possible race condition):", e);
        }
    }
});

Once enough candidates are exchanged and a path is confirmed, pc.iceConnectionState transitions to "connected". The peer-to-peer channel is live.

Race Condition Handling

A race condition is when two things happen in parallel and the second one assumes the first is already done — but it isn't yet. In WebRTC, socket messages and browser async operations run independently of each other. They do not wait for each other. That independence is what causes races.

Three specific races can break a WebRTC connection silently — meaning no visible error, just a connection that never works. Each one has a fix that is a few lines of code once you understand what is happening.

Race 1 — ICE Candidates Arriving Before Remote Description

The situation in plain English: The Caller finishes generating their offer and immediately starts discovering network paths (ICE candidates). Those candidates get sent over the socket right away. But on the Callee's side, processing the offer takes a moment — it is async. So the candidates can land on the Callee before the Callee has even started processing the offer. When the Callee then tries to use those candidates, the connection has no idea what they are for. The candidates are thrown away. Permanently.

  TIMELINE — What the race looks like

  Caller                                    Callee
  ──────                                    ──────
  setLocalDescription(offer)
    └─> ICE gathering starts immediately
  socket.emit("offer")           ─────────────────────────> [sits in queue]
  socket.emit("ice-candidate")   ─────────────────────────> ARRIVES FIRST
  socket.emit("ice-candidate")   ─────────────────────────> ARRIVES SECOND
  socket.emit("ice-candidate")   ─────────────────────────> ARRIVES THIRD
                                            ↓
                                  Callee tries addIceCandidate()
                                  but remoteDescription is still null
                                  → THROWS. Candidates are gone forever.
                                            ↓
                                  [offer event finally fires]
                                  setRemoteDescription(offer)
                                  ← too late. Those candidates are lost.

The mental model for the fix — a waiting room: Think of the queue as a waiting room. When ICE candidates arrive and the connection is not ready for them yet, they sit in the waiting room and wait. The moment the connection becomes ready (i.e., setRemoteDescription() finishes), you open the door and let all the waiting candidates in at once. After that, any new candidates that arrive go straight in — the waiting room is no longer needed.

  QUEUE STAGES — Mental model

  Stage 1: WAITING ROOM EMPTY, CONNECTION NOT READY YET
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  remoteDescription = null
  pendingCandidates["socketA"] = []          ← waiting room exists but is empty

  Stage 2: CANDIDATES ARRIVE, CONNECTION STILL NOT READY
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ice-candidate arrives → remoteDescription still null → goes into waiting room
  pendingCandidates["socketA"] = [cand1]

  ice-candidate arrives → remoteDescription still null → waiting room
  pendingCandidates["socketA"] = [cand1, cand2]

  ice-candidate arrives → remoteDescription still null → waiting room
  pendingCandidates["socketA"] = [cand1, cand2, cand3]

  Stage 3: CONNECTION BECOMES READY (setRemoteDescription resolves)
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  remoteDescription is now set ✓
  flushPendingCandidates("socketA") is called immediately
    → addIceCandidate(cand1) ✓
    → addIceCandidate(cand2) ✓
    → addIceCandidate(cand3) ✓
  pendingCandidates["socketA"] = []          ← waiting room cleared

  Stage 4: CONNECTION READY, NEW CANDIDATES BYPASS WAITING ROOM
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  ice-candidate arrives → remoteDescription is set → addIceCandidate() directly ✓

Now here is the code that implements exactly this, broken into three parts.

Part A — The waiting room data structure. A Map is used because you have one queue per remote user, not one global queue. If you are in a room with three others, you have three separate waiting rooms, one per socketId.

Part A — Declare the queueJavaScript
// This Map holds a list of candidates for each remote peer.
// Key:   the remote user's socketId (a string like "XkP2q...")
// Value: an array of ICE candidate objects that arrived too early
//
// Example of what it looks like mid-race:
//   pendingCandidates = {
//     "XkP2q..." => [candidate1, candidate2, candidate3],
//     "Rm9zL..." => [candidate1]
//   }
const pendingCandidates = new Map();

Part B — The ICE candidate handler with the queue logic. Every time a candidate arrives, you ask one question: is the connection ready for it? If yes, apply it directly. If no, put it in the waiting room.

Part B — ICE candidate handler: apply or queueJavaScript
socket.on("ice-candidate", async ({ candidate, senderSocketId }) => {

    // Look up the connection for the person who sent this candidate.
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);

    // Safety check: if we have no PC for this person, or the candidate
    // itself is null (the browser sends a null at the end of gathering),
    // just ignore it and stop here.
    if (!pc || !candidate) return;

    // THE KEY QUESTION: does this PC already have a remote description?
    // pc.remoteDescription is null until setRemoteDescription() has been called.
    // If it is NOT null, the connection is ready — apply the candidate now.
    if (pc.remoteDescription) {

        // Safe path: the offer/answer has been exchanged, so the PC knows
        // what this connection is. It can accept the candidate immediately.
        try {
            await pc.addIceCandidate(new RTCIceCandidate(candidate));
        } catch (e) {
            console.warn("ICE add failed:", e);
        }

    } else {

        // Too-early path: pc.remoteDescription is still null.
        // We cannot use this candidate yet — put it in the waiting room.

        // If there is no waiting room for this person yet, create one.
        // (This runs on the very first early candidate from that peer.)
        if (!pendingCandidates.has(senderSocketId))
            pendingCandidates.set(senderSocketId, []);

        // Add this candidate to their waiting room array.
        // It will sit here until flushPendingCandidates() is called.
        pendingCandidates.get(senderSocketId).push(candidate);
    }
});

Part C — The flush function. This is called the moment setRemoteDescription() finishes. It empties the waiting room by feeding all held candidates into the now-ready connection, one by one.

Part C — flushPendingCandidates: drain the waiting roomJavaScript
async function flushPendingCandidates(socketId) {

    // Get the connection we are flushing into.
    const pc = pcMap.get(socketId);

    // Get all candidates that were stored in the waiting room.
    // The "?? []" means: if there is no entry for this socketId at all
    // (no race happened, nobody was queued), use an empty array instead.
    // That way the function is always safe to call, even if the queue is empty.
    const queue = pendingCandidates.get(socketId) ?? [];

    // IMPORTANT: delete the waiting room BEFORE processing candidates.
    // Why? Because addIceCandidate() is async. If a new candidate arrives
    // while we are in the middle of flushing, the handler above would see
    // that remoteDescription is now set and take the safe path — no double-queue.
    pendingCandidates.delete(socketId);

    // Now loop through every candidate that was waiting and apply them.
    // We use "for...of" (not forEach) because we need to await each one
    // sequentially — addIceCandidate is async and order can matter.
    for (const candidate of queue) {
        try {
            await pc.addIceCandidate(new RTCIceCandidate(candidate));
        } catch (e) {
            // A candidate failing here is usually harmless — the browser
            // will find other paths. Log it but do not stop the loop.
            console.warn("ICE flush error on one candidate:", e);
        }
    }
    // After this function returns, the waiting room is gone and all
    // candidates have been applied. The race window is closed.
}

Part D — Wiring the flush into the offer and answer handlers. The one rule: call flushPendingCandidates() immediately after every setRemoteDescription() call. There are two places where setRemoteDescription() is called — the offer handler (Callee side) and the answer handler (Caller side). Both need the flush.

Part D — Updated offer and answer handlers with flushJavaScript
// CALLEE SIDE — receives the offer, processes it, sends back an answer.
socket.on("offer", async ({ offer, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);

    // Step 1: Apply the offer. This sets remoteDescription on the PC.
    // After this line, the PC knows who it is talking to.
    await pc.setRemoteDescription(new RTCSessionDescription(offer));

    // Step 2: Flush immediately. setRemoteDescription just resolved,
    // so the PC can now accept candidates. Empty the waiting room now.
    // Any candidates that arrived before the offer are applied here.
    await flushPendingCandidates(senderSocketId);

    // Step 3: Create and send the answer. Continue as normal.
    const answer = await pc.createAnswer();
    await pc.setLocalDescription(answer);
    socket.emit("answer", { answer, targetSocketId: senderSocketId });
});

// CALLER SIDE — receives the answer, completes its own SDP setup.
socket.on("answer", async ({ answer, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);
    if (!pc) return;

    // Step 1: Apply the answer. This sets remoteDescription on the Caller's PC.
    await pc.setRemoteDescription(new RTCSessionDescription(answer));

    // Step 2: Flush here too. The Caller's PC also receives ICE candidates
    // from the Callee that might have arrived before this answer was processed.
    await flushPendingCandidates(senderSocketId);
});

Race 2 — Concurrent onnegotiationneeded Triggers

The situation in plain English: Imagine you add your audio track and your video track to the connection at the same time (in a loop). The browser sees each addTrack() call and thinks: "something changed, I need to renegotiate" — so it fires onnegotiationneeded once for audio and once for video. Your handler sends a new offer on the first trigger. But the second trigger fires almost immediately, while the first offer is still mid-flight. You try to create a second offer and the browser stops you: "you already have an offer in flight, signalingState is "have-local-offer", not "stable""— and it throws.

Why you do not need to retry: The browser is smarter than it seems here. Once the first offer-answer cycle completes and signalingState returns to "stable", the browser automatically re-fires onnegotiationneeded one more time — this time with the full picture of all tracks that changed. You do not need to track what you missed. You just need to skip the bad trigger.

  WHAT HAPPENS WITHOUT THE GUARD

  addTrack(audioTrack) fires onnegotiationneeded
    → signalingState = "stable" → send offer ✓ → signalingState = "have-local-offer"

  addTrack(videoTrack) fires onnegotiationneeded (milliseconds later)
    → signalingState = "have-local-offer" → try to send another offer
    → setLocalDescription() THROWS because an offer is already in flight ✗

  WHAT HAPPENS WITH THE GUARD

  addTrack(audioTrack) fires onnegotiationneeded
    → signalingState = "stable" → send offer ✓ → signalingState = "have-local-offer"

  addTrack(videoTrack) fires onnegotiationneeded
    → signalingState = "have-local-offer" → guard triggers → return early (skip)

  ... offer is answered, cycle completes, signalingState = "stable" again ...

  Browser re-fires onnegotiationneeded automatically
    → signalingState = "stable" → send offer ✓ (now includes both audio + video)
onnegotiationneeded — the signalingState guard explainedJavaScript
pc.onnegotiationneeded = async () => {

    // Before doing anything, check whether the connection is in a
    // "calm" state. "stable" means: no offer is currently in flight,
    // and we are not in the middle of processing someone else's offer.
    // It is the only state where creating a new offer is safe.
    if (pc.signalingState !== "stable") {

        // An offer is already being negotiated. Skip this trigger.
        // Do NOT retry. Do NOT queue this. Just return.
        // The browser will fire onnegotiationneeded again once
        // the current cycle completes and signalingState = "stable".
        console.log("Negotiation skipped — already negotiating. State:", pc.signalingState);
        return;
    }

    // Safe to proceed. signalingState is "stable", so we are the only
    // offer in flight. Send it.
    await sendOfferTo(remoteUser, pc);
};

Race 3 — Stale Answer in Wrong Signaling State

The situation in plain English: This one is rarer but more dramatic. In edge cases — a user's page reloads mid-call, a socket reconnects and replays old events, or a server glitch sends a duplicate message — an "answer" socket event arrives when your PC is not actually waiting for one. Maybe you already got the answer and your signalingState is back to "stable", or maybe something went wrong and the PC was closed. Calling setRemoteDescription(answer) when the state is not "have-local-offer" throws an error. The guard is a single if-check that says: "if I am not actively waiting for an answer right now, ignore this."

  NORMAL FLOW (no race)                     EDGE CASE (stale answer)
  ─────────────────────────────────         ───────────────────────────────────────
  signalingState = "stable"                 signalingState = "stable"
  sendOffer()                               sendOffer()
  signalingState = "have-local-offer"       signalingState = "have-local-offer"
  answer arrives → correct state ✓          answer arrives → applied ✓
  setRemoteDescription(answer)              signalingState = "stable"
  signalingState = "stable"
                                            [network glitch: same answer resent]
                                            answer arrives AGAIN
                                            signalingState = "stable" ← not expecting one
                                            setRemoteDescription() → THROWS ✗
                                                  ↓
                                            guard catches it → return early ✓
Defensive answer handler — state guard explainedJavaScript
socket.on("answer", async ({ answer, senderSocketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(senderSocketId);

    // If there is no PC for this person at all (e.g., they left and
    // we cleaned up), just ignore the message entirely.
    if (!pc) return;

    // "have-local-offer" is the ONLY state where receiving an answer makes sense.
    // It means: we sent an offer and we are actively waiting for their answer.
    // Any other state — "stable", "have-remote-offer", "closed" — means
    // this answer is unexpected. Applying it would throw. Ignore it instead.
    if (pc.signalingState !== "have-local-offer") {
        console.warn("Unexpected answer received. Current state:", pc.signalingState, "— ignoring.");
        return; // Do nothing. Do not throw. Just quietly discard it.
    }

    // We ARE in "have-local-offer" — this is the answer we expected. Apply it.
    await pc.setRemoteDescription(new RTCSessionDescription(answer));

    // And as always: flush any candidates that were waiting for this moment.
    await flushPendingCandidates(senderSocketId);
});
✓

All three guards together — pending candidate queue (Race 1), signalingState check in onnegotiationneeded (Race 2), and state guard before setRemoteDescription (Race 3) — cover the vast majority of race conditions encountered in production WebRTC apps. Add all three from the start. They are cheap to write and extremely expensive to debug after the fact.

Step 6 — Media Flows Automatically

Once the connection is established, if either peer has called pc.addTrack() at any point, the remote peer's pc.ontrack fires automatically with the incoming stream. No additional code is needed on the receiver side — the handler attached in Step 2 takes care of it.

  Either peer calls pc.addTrack(track, stream)
         |
         v
  onnegotiationneeded fires automatically on that PC
         |
         v
  sendOfferTo() runs → new offer-answer cycle
         |
         v
  ICE completes (or reuses existing path)
         |
         v
  Remote peer's pc.ontrack fires automatically
         |
         v
  Remote video element updated — media is visible

Mesh Topology

In a room with N users, each user maintains a direct peer connection to every other user. This is called a full mesh. Every user has (N-1) RTCPeerConnection objects, and the total connections in the room grows as N*(N-1)/2.

  4-user room — each line represents one RTCPeerConnection

         A
        / \
       /   \
      B─────C
       \   /
        \ /
         D

  A's pcMap: { B, C, D }    (3 connections)
  B's pcMap: { A, C, D }    (3 connections)
  C's pcMap: { A, B, D }    (3 connections)
  D's pcMap: { A, B, C }    (3 connections)

  Total unique connections: 6

Mesh is the simplest architecture and works well for small rooms (up to roughly 4–6 users). For larger rooms, a Selective Forwarding Unit (SFU) server is used — each user sends one stream to the server and the server distributes it. SFU is a production-scale infrastructure concern beyond the scope of this guide.

N Users Joining — Complete Sequence

The following traces the exact event sequence when C joins a room where A and B are already connected to each other.

  A and B already have a connection between them. C joins.

  C's socket           Server              A's socket          B's socket
  ────────             ──────              ──────────          ──────────
  emit("join-room") -> registers C
                       get-others ──> [A,B] ──> to C only
                       user-joined ──────────────────────────> to A
                       user-joined ─────────────────────────────────────> to B

  C receives [A, B]:              A receives user-joined=C:    B receives user-joined=C:
    createPCWith(A)                 createPCWith(C)              createPCWith(C)
    sendOfferTo(A) ─offer──────>    [waits for C's offer]        [waits for C's offer]
    createPCWith(B)
    sendOfferTo(B) ─offer──────────────────────────────────>

                                  A receives offer from C:     B receives offer from C:
                                    setRemoteDescription         setRemoteDescription
                                    createAnswer                 createAnswer
                                    setLocalDescription          setLocalDescription
                                    emit("answer") ──────────>   emit("answer") ──────────>

  C receives answer from A:
    setRemoteDescription(answerA)
  C receives answer from B:
    setRemoteDescription(answerB)

  ICE candidates exchanged in parallel on all three pairs: C↔A, C↔B
  (A↔B connection already exists and is unaffected)

  Final pcMap state:
    A:  { B.socketId => PC(A-B),  C.socketId => PC(A-C) }
    B:  { A.socketId => PC(A-B),  C.socketId => PC(B-C) }
    C:  { A.socketId => PC(A-C),  B.socketId => PC(B-C) }
✓

A and B's existing connection is completely unaffected when C joins. Each connection is independent. Adding a user means forming new pairs — not disrupting or rebuilding existing ones.

User Leaving

Two teardown paths must be handled: when you leave, and when someone else leaves while you remain.

When you leave

1
Close all peer connections

Iterate over pcMap, call pc.close() on each, then clear the map.

2
Stop all local tracks

Call track.stop() on each active track. This releases the camera and microphone hardware. Failing to do this leaves the browser's recording indicator active.

3
Notify server

Emit a leave-room event so the server notifies others, then disconnect the socket.

When someone else leaves

socket.on("user-left")JavaScript
socket.on("user-left", ({ socketId }) => {
    const pc = pcMap.get(socketId);
    if (pc) { pc.close(); pcMap.delete(socketId); }
    userMap.delete(socketId);
    removeUserFromUI(socketId);
});

Tracks and Streams

A MediaStreamTrack is a single source of media — one audio channel or one video channel. A MediaStream groups one or more tracks together. You interact with tracks in two contexts: getting them from hardware, and adding them to peer connections to send to remote peers.

Acquiring tracks from hardwareJavaScript
// Camera and microphone
const camStream = await navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia({ video: true, audio: true });
const videoTrack = camStream.getVideoTracks()[0];
const audioTrack = camStream.getAudioTracks()[0];

// Screen capture
const screenStream = await navigator.mediaDevices.getDisplayMedia({ video: true });
const screenTrack = screenStream.getVideoTracks()[0];

Once you have a track, add it to every PC in pcMap using pc.addTrack(track, stream). Each connection is independent — you must call this on every PC. Adding a track to a connected PC triggers onnegotiationneeded automatically.

Starting With No Tracks

The correct default state for a video call app: user joins with camera off, mic off, screen not shared. No permission prompts on join, no media acquired until the user explicitly enables something.

WebRTC handles this gracefully. An RTCPeerConnection can be established between two users even if neither has any tracks. The connection reaches "connected" state with no media flowing. When either user enables a track later, onnegotiationneeded fires and the SDP updates automatically.

💡

The connection is a channel. Tracks are what you choose to send through it. You can add tracks, remove tracks, and swap tracks — the channel stays open throughout. The connection lifecycle and the track lifecycle are independent.

  At join time (correct default):

  User A <────── connected, zero tracks ──────> User B
                 iceConnectionState = "connected"
                 signalingState = "stable"
                 No audio, no video flowing.

  After A enables camera:

  User A <────── connected + video track ──────> User B
                 B's pc.ontrack fires automatically
                 B sees A's video

Toggling Tracks On and Off

Two operations are both commonly called "toggling", but they behave differently in WebRTC.

track.enabled = false / true

Pauses the track without removing it from the connection. No renegotiation. The slot in the SDP stays reserved. Re-enabling is instant. Use this for muting mic or temporarily disabling camera.

pc.removeTrack(sender)

Fully removes the track from the connection. onnegotiationneeded fires and a new offer-answer cycle runs. Use this when stopping screen share and switching back to camera.

Toggle mic — enabled flag pattern

toggleAudio()JavaScript
async function toggleAudio() {
    if (audioTrack) {
        // Track exists: flip enabled. No renegotiation needed.
        audioTrack.enabled = !audioTrack.enabled;
    } else {
        // First time: get permission and add to all connections.
        const stream = await navigator.mediaDevices.getUserMedia({ audio: true });
        audioTrack = stream.getAudioTracks()[0];
        pcMap.forEach((pc) => pc.addTrack(audioTrack, stream));
        // onnegotiationneeded fires on each PC → renegotiation is automatic.
    }
}

Toggle screen share — removeTrack pattern

toggleScreen()JavaScript
async function toggleScreen() {
    if (screenTrack) {
        // Stop: remove from all connections, then release hardware.
        pcMap.forEach((pc) => {
            const sender = pc.getSenders().find((s) => s.track === screenTrack);
            if (sender) pc.removeTrack(sender);
        });
        screenTrack.stop();
        screenTrack = null;
    } else {
        // Start: acquire and add to all connections.
        const stream = await navigator.mediaDevices.getDisplayMedia({ video: true });
        screenTrack = stream.getVideoTracks()[0];
        pcMap.forEach((pc) => pc.addTrack(screenTrack, stream));
        // Handle user clicking browser's native "Stop sharing" button.
        screenTrack.onended = () => toggleScreen();
    }
}

New Joiner Sees Existing Tracks

When C joins and A already has their camera on, C must see A's video immediately upon connection — without A needing to do anything special. This requires one specific action: when A creates a new PC for C, A must add their currently-active tracks to that PC right at creation time.

💡

Each RTCPeerConnection is independent. A track being on one PC does not mean it is on all PCs. When a new PC is created, it starts empty. You must explicitly add any currently-active tracks to it at the moment of creation.

createPCWith() — add active tracks to new connectionJavaScript
function createPCWith(remoteUser) {
    const pc = new RTCPeerConnection({ iceServers: [...] });
    pcMap.set(remoteUser.socketId, pc);

    // ... attach event listeners ...

    // Add any tracks currently active to this new connection.
    // This is what makes a new joiner see existing streams immediately.
    if (audioTrack) pc.addTrack(audioTrack, audioStream);
    if (videoTrack) pc.addTrack(videoTrack, videoStream);
    if (screenTrack) pc.addTrack(screenTrack, screenStream);
    // Adding these triggers onnegotiationneeded → the SDP offer will include them.

    return pc;
}
  A has camera on. C joins. A receives "user-joined" for C.

  A calls createPCWith(C):
    videoTrack exists → pc.addTrack(videoTrack, videoStream)
    onnegotiationneeded fires
    sendOfferTo(C) → offer SDP includes the video track description

  C receives offer (SDP says "I have a video track"):
    setRemoteDescription(offer)
    createAnswer, setLocalDescription, emit answer

  ICE completes. C's pc.ontrack fires. C sees A's video.
  No extra action from A. No action from C beyond answering.

Renegotiation

Renegotiation is a new offer-answer cycle on an already-connected peer connection. It is triggered automatically by the browser whenever the set of tracks changes — when addTrack() or removeTrack() is called on a connected PC. Your onnegotiationneeded handler runs sendOfferTo(), the remote side answers, and the SDP is updated to reflect the new track state.

  RENEGOTIATION SEQUENCE (mid-call track enable)

  You call pc.addTrack(track, stream)
         |
         v
  pc.onnegotiationneeded fires automatically
         |
         v
  your handler checks pc.signalingState === "stable"  (guard)
         |
         v
  sendOfferTo(remoteUser, pc) → new offer-answer cycle
         |
         v
  Remote peer receives new offer, answers it
         |
         v
  ICE may reuse existing path (usually no new ICE needed)
         |
         v
  Remote peer's pc.ontrack fires with the new track
⚠

onnegotiationneeded can fire multiple times in quick succession if multiple tracks are added in a loop. Always check pc.signalingState === "stable" before proceeding inside the handler — if a previous offer is already in flight, skip this trigger.

✓

Renegotiation is what makes the entire toggle system work without any special handling on the receiver side. The receiver does not know a track was toggled — they receive a new offer, answer it, and their ontrack fires with whatever changed. This is the design elegance of WebRTC when used correctly.
