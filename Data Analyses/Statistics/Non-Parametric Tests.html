Section 01

Why Non-Parametric Tests?

Before we learn what non-parametric tests are, we need to understand the problem they solve. To do that, let's talk about their older sibling — parametric tests.

Definition · Parametric Tests

Parametric tests are statistical procedures that make assumptions about the underlying population distribution from which the sample was drawn — most commonly that the data follows a Normal (Gaussian) distribution, and that the population variance is known or estimated.

Examples: t-test, ANOVA, Pearson correlation, z-test.

These assumptions are powerful — they let us derive precise mathematical formulas and make strong inferences. The word parametric comes from the fact that the tests estimate parameters of a known distribution (like the mean μ and standard deviation σ of a normal curve). But this power comes with a price: if the data doesn't actually follow the assumed distribution, the test's conclusions can be completely wrong.

Consider a t-test comparing the average salary of employees at two companies. If one company has a CEO earning ₹50 crore/year alongside employees earning ₹5 lakh/year, the mean salary is dragged far above what any typical employee earns. A t-test on this data would be comparing these heavily-distorted means — not the actual experience of the workforce. This is where non-parametric tests shine.

⚠ When Parametric Tests Fail

  • 🔹 Your data is severely skewed (e.g., income data, hospital wait times)
  • 🔹 You have ordinal data — rankings, Likert scales (Strongly Agree → Strongly Disagree)
  • 🔹 Small sample sizes that don't allow you to verify normality
  • 🔹 Your data contains significant outliers that distort the mean
  • 🔹 You're measuring something where the average doesn't make sense (e.g., ranking a movie 1–5)

Definition · Non-Parametric Tests

Non-parametric tests (also called distribution-free tests) are statistical procedures that make no assumptions about the shape of the population distribution. They work by analysing the rank order of observations rather than their exact numerical values, making them robust to outliers and applicable to ordinal data.

💡 Intuition — The Street Racing Analogy

Imagine you're comparing two racing teams. A parametric test says: "Tell me the exact speeds of every car, and I'll compute averages." But what if a GPS malfunctioned and you only know the finishing order?

A non-parametric test says: "Fine — I only need to know who came 1st, 2nd, 3rd..." It works with ranks, not raw numbers. Less precise? Slightly. But robust and always valid.

Section 02

The Core Mechanism: Ranking

Almost every non-parametric test operates by converting raw data into ranks. Understanding this process is the master key to understanding all the tests that follow. The beauty of ranking is that it strips away the actual magnitude of the numbers and retains only their relative order. A score of 1,000,000 and a score of 51 are just "rank 10" and "rank 9" — the outlier loses its power to distort the analysis.

Definition · Ranking

Ranking is the process of replacing each observation with its position in the sorted order of all observations. The smallest value receives rank 1, the next smallest rank 2, and so on. When two values are identical (called ties), they receive the average of the ranks they would have occupied.

💡 Intuition — The Exam Score Analogy

Suppose 5 students scored: 88, 45, 62, 91, 62 on an exam. Instead of working with these raw scores, we ask: "What position does each student hold when everyone lines up from lowest to highest?" The student who scored 45 is at the back of the line — rank 1. The two students who both scored 62 share positions 2 and 3, so they each get rank (2+3)/2 = 2.5. The student with 88 is rank 4, and 91 is rank 5 at the front.

Notice: even if that top student scored 991 instead of 91, they'd still just be rank 5. The extreme value has no special influence.

Now compare the rank sums or statistics between groups.

Section 03

Mann-Whitney U Test

Non-parametric alternative to the independent samples t-test

Definition

The Mann-Whitney U test (also known as the Wilcoxon Rank-Sum test) determines whether there is a statistically significant difference in the distribution of two independent groups. It tests whether one group tends to produce larger values than the other, using only the rank ordering of observations.

Use when: two independent groups, ordinal or non-normal continuous data. The groups must be independent — the same subject cannot appear in both groups.

The word independent here is crucial. Independent means the two groups have no connection — different people, no matching, no pairing. If you measure the same person twice (before and after), you use a different test (Wilcoxon Signed-Rank). Think of Mann-Whitney as answering: "If I randomly pick one person from each group, who is likely to score higher?"

💡 Intuition — The U Statistic Explained

Consider every possible pair: one person from Group A and one from Group B. For each pair, note who scores higher. U₁ counts the number of pairs where Group A beats Group B. U₂ counts the pairs where Group B beats Group A. If the groups are equal, U₁ ≈ U₂ ≈ n₁·n₂/2.

The test statistic U = min(U₁, U₂). A very small U means one group almost always beats the other — strong evidence of a real difference. The maximum possible U is n₁·n₂ (when one group wins every single pairing). We use the minimum because we're doing a two-tailed test, looking for extreme values in either direction.

📚 Real Example: Drug Trial

A pharmaceutical researcher tests whether a new painkiller reduces pain scores (scale 1–10) compared to a placebo. Pain scores are skewed (many low scores, few high), so a t-test is unreliable.

Drug Group (n=6)

Scores: 2, 3, 3, 5, 4, 2

Placebo Group (n=6)

Scores: 6, 7, 5, 8, 6, 7

The Mann-Whitney U test will tell us whether the drug group systematically scores lower (less pain) than the placebo group.

Step 3 — Calculate U Statistics

U₁ = n₁·n₂ + [n₁(n₁+1)/2] − R₁ = 6×6 + [6×7/2] − 21.5 = 36 + 21 − 21.5 = 35.5

U₂ = n₁·n₂ − U₁ = 36 − 35.5 = 0.5

U = min(U₁, U₂) = min(35.5, 0.5) = 0.5

A U of 0.5 is extremely small. It means in virtually every possible Drug vs Placebo pairing, the Drug patient scored lower.

Step 4 — Compare to Critical Value

For n₁ = n₂ = 6 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed), the critical value from the Mann-Whitney table is U_crit = 5.

Our U = 0.5 < 5 = U_crit → Reject H₀

✅ Conclusion

There is a statistically significant difference between the Drug and Placebo groups (U = 0.5, p < 0.05). The drug group consistently reported lower pain scores. The painkiller is effective compared to placebo.

⚠ Common Misconception

The Mann-Whitney U test does not compare medians (a widespread myth). It compares the entire distribution of the two groups. It answers: "Does one group tend to produce stochastically larger values?" — not just whether the medians differ.

Section 04

Wilcoxon Signed-Rank Test

Non-parametric alternative to the paired samples t-test

Definition

The Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test assesses whether the median difference between paired observations is zero. Unlike the Mann-Whitney U test (for independent groups), this test is for situations where the same subjects are measured twice — typically before and after a treatment — or two matched conditions are compared.

Use when: paired/matched data, non-normal differences.

💡 Intuition — The "Did Things Change?" Test

Compute the difference (Before − After) for each person. If the programme has no real effect, some patients should improve slightly and others might get slightly worse — the differences would be mixed, positive and negative, roughly cancelling out. The Wilcoxon test formalises this: it ranks the magnitude of each difference, then checks whether the positive differences dominate the negative ones. If the treatment works, most differences will be in one direction, giving a lopsided rank sum.

📚 Real Example: Blood Pressure Treatment

8 hypertensive patients have their systolic blood pressure measured before and after a 4-week dietary programme. We want to know: did the programme significantly lower blood pressure? Since differences may not be normally distributed (and n is small), we use the Wilcoxon Signed-Rank test.

✏ Worked Solution — Step by Step

Step 1 — Compute the Differences (Before − After)

P1: 152−140 = 12  |  P2: 148−135 = 13  |  P3: 160−145 = 15  |  P4: 145−142 = 3

P5: 155−150 = 5  |  P6: 162−147 = 15  |  P7: 140−138 = 2  |  P8: 150−142 = 8

All differences are positive — blood pressure dropped for every patient. This is already a strong signal.

Step 2 — Rank the Absolute Differences (smallest = rank 1)

Sorted |diffs|: 2 (P7), 3 (P4), 5 (P5), 8 (P8), 12 (P1), 13 (P2), 15 (P3), 15 (P6)

Ranks: P7→1, P4→2, P5→3, P8→4, P1→5, P2→6, P3 & P6 tie at positions 7 & 8 → each gets (7+8)/2 = 7.5

Step 3 — Apply Signs & Sum

All differences are positive (BP decreased for everyone), so all signed ranks are positive:

W⁺ = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6 + 7.5 + 7.5 = 36

W⁻ = 0 (no negative differences exist)

W = min(W⁺, W⁻) = min(36, 0) = 0

Step 4 — Compare to Critical Value

For n = 8 at α = 0.05 (two-tailed), W_crit = 4.

Our W = 0 < 4 = W_crit → Reject H₀

The smaller the W, the stronger the evidence. W = 0 is the most extreme possible outcome — it means every single difference pointed in the same direction.

✅ Conclusion

The dietary programme produced a statistically significant reduction in systolic blood pressure (W = 0, p < 0.05). Every patient's BP decreased, and the magnitude of those decreases was consistently large. We reject H₀ and conclude the programme is effective.

Formula

W = min(W⁺, W⁻)

W⁺ = Σ Rᵢ for all positive differences
W⁻ = Σ Rᵢ for all negative differences

Rᵢ = rank of |dᵢ| among all non-zero |dᵢ|

A very small W (close to 0) suggests the treatment had a consistent effect in one direction → reject H₀.

Section 05

Kruskal-Wallis Test

Non-parametric alternative to one-way ANOVA (3+ groups)

Definition

The Kruskal-Wallis H test is an extension of the Mann-Whitney U test to three or more independent groups. It tests whether the samples originate from the same distribution by examining whether the rank sums across groups differ more than would be expected by chance. It is the non-parametric equivalent of a one-way ANOVA.

💡 Intuition — The "Which Group Ranks Highest?" Question

Think of it this way: you have 15 students spread across three classrooms. You rank all 15 by their score (rank 1 = lowest scorer overall, rank 15 = highest). Now count up the total ranks earned by each classroom. If all three teaching methods were equally effective, you'd expect each classroom to hold roughly similar rank totals — no classroom would dominate the high or low end. But if one classroom earns most of the top ranks, that's evidence its method produces better results. The H statistic captures exactly this — how unevenly the ranks are distributed across the groups.

📚 Real Example: Teaching Methods

A school compares exam scores across three teaching methods: Traditional, Online, and Hybrid. There are 5 students per group (N = 15 total). The score distributions are non-normal, so ANOVA is inappropriate.

Traditional (Group 1)

55, 58, 60, 65, 72

Online (Group 2)

75, 78, 82, 88, 90

Hybrid (Group 3)

65, 68, 70, 76, 80

Step 2 — Sum the Ranks per Group (Rⱼ)

R₁ (Traditional) = 1 + 2 + 3 + 4.5 + 8 = 18.5

R₂ (Online) = 9 + 11 + 13 + 14 + 15 = 62

R₃ (Hybrid) = 4.5 + 6 + 7 + 10 + 12 = 39.5

✓ Check: 18.5 + 62 + 39.5 = 120 = N(N+1)/2 = 15×16/2 ✓

Step 3 — Compute the H Statistic

H = [12 / N(N+1)] × Σ[Rⱼ²/nⱼ] − 3(N+1)

Σ[Rⱼ²/nⱼ] = (18.5²/5) + (62²/5) + (39.5²/5)

            = (342.25/5) + (3844/5) + (1560.25/5)

            = 68.45 + 768.8 + 312.05 = 1149.3

H = [12 / (15×16)] × 1149.3 − 3×16

H = [12/240] × 1149.3 − 48

H = 0.05 × 1149.3 − 48 = 57.465 − 48 = 9.47

Step 4 — Compare to χ² Critical Value

Degrees of freedom = k − 1 = 3 − 1 = 2

At α = 0.05, χ²_crit (df=2) = 5.991 (from chi-square table)

H = 9.47 > 5.991 → Reject H₀

✅ Conclusion

There is a statistically significant difference in exam scores across the three teaching methods (H = 9.47, p < 0.05). The Online method students consistently occupied the highest ranks. A post-hoc Mann-Whitney U test with Bonferroni correction (α' = 0.05/3 ≈ 0.017) would identify which specific pairs differ significantly.

Formula

H = [12 / N(N+1)] × Σ [Rⱼ² / nⱼ] − 3(N+1)
N

Total number of observations across all groups

nⱼ

Number of obs in group j

Rⱼ

Sum of ranks in group j

H follows a chi-square distribution with k−1 degrees of freedom (k = number of groups). Large H → reject H₀.

⚠ Post-Hoc Testing

A significant Kruskal-Wallis result only tells you that at least one group differs — it doesn't say which. To find the specific pairs, you run post-hoc Mann-Whitney U tests with a Bonferroni correction (divide α by the number of comparisons).

Section 06

Spearman's Rank Correlation (ρ)

Non-parametric alternative to Pearson's r

Definition

Spearman's rank-order correlation coefficient (ρ, "rho") measures the strength and direction of the monotonic relationship between two variables by computing Pearson's r on the ranks of the data, rather than the raw values. A monotonic relationship means as one variable increases, the other consistently increases (or decreases) — but not necessarily at a constant rate.

💡 Monotonic vs Linear — The Key Distinction

Pearson's r (parametric) measures linear relationships: the data must increase at a constant rate. Spearman's ρ only requires that the relationship is monotonic — consistently going up or down, even if the rate changes. This is why Spearman handles exponential growth, logarithmic curves, and ordinal rankings perfectly well.

📚 Real Example: Study Hours vs Class Rank

A professor tracks 5 students' weekly study hours and their final class rank (1 = top of class). Does studying more correlate with a better class rank? Note: "class rank" is ordinal — Spearman is the correct choice over Pearson here.

StudentHours/Week (X)Class Rank (Y)Rank(X)Rank(Y)*dᵢ = Rank(X)−Rank(Y)dᵢ²
A152nd34-11
B85th1100
C201st5500
D123rd23-11
E184th4224

*Rank(Y): class rank 5th is the worst → assigned the lowest performance rank (1). 1st place is best → assigned rank 5. We re-rank so that higher rank = better performance, making the direction intuitive.

✏ Worked Solution — Step by Step

Step 1 — Rank Both Variables Independently

Rank X (study hours) in ascending order: 8→1, 12→2, 15→3, 18→4, 20→5

Rank Y (performance): re-rank class position so better outcome = higher rank. 5th place→1, 4th→2, 3rd→3, 2nd→4, 1st→5

Step 2 — Compute Differences (dᵢ) and Square Them

A: Rank(X)=3, Rank(Y)=4 → d = −1, d² = 1

B: Rank(X)=1, Rank(Y)=1 → d = 0, d² = 0

C: Rank(X)=5, Rank(Y)=5 → d = 0, d² = 0

D: Rank(X)=2, Rank(Y)=3 → d = −1, d² = 1

E: Rank(X)=4, Rank(Y)=2 → d = 2, d² = 4

Σdᵢ² = 1 + 0 + 0 + 1 + 4 = 6

Step 3 — Apply the Spearman Formula

ρ = 1 − [6·Σdᵢ²] / [n(n²−1)]

ρ = 1 − [6 × 6] / [5 × (25−1)]

ρ = 1 − 36 / [5 × 24]

ρ = 1 − 36/120 = 1 − 0.30 = +0.70

Step 4 — Interpret the Result

ρ = +0.70 indicates a strong positive monotonic relationship. Students who study more tend to achieve better class positions. The relationship is not perfect (ρ ≠ 1), but it is consistent and meaningful.

For n = 5 at α = 0.05, the critical value for Spearman's ρ ≈ 0.900 (very small samples need very high ρ to reach significance). With larger samples this ρ = 0.70 would be highly significant.

✅ Conclusion

There is a strong positive monotonic association between weekly study hours and academic class rank (ρ = 0.70). Students who invest more study time consistently rank higher — though the small sample size (n=5) limits formal significance testing here. With a larger cohort, this effect would almost certainly be statistically significant.

Formula

ρ = 1 − [6·Σdᵢ²] / [n(n²−1)]

where dᵢ = rank(xᵢ) − rank(yᵢ)

Interpretation of ρ

  • ρ = +1.0 → Perfect positive monotonic
  • ρ = 0.7–1.0 → Strong positive
  • ρ = 0 → No relationship
  • ρ = −0.7 to −1.0 → Strong negative
  • ρ = −1.0 → Perfect negative monotonic

Pearson vs Spearman

  • Use Pearson: normally distributed data, linear relationship
  • Use Spearman: ordinal data, outliers present, non-linear monotonic trend

Section 07

Chi-Square Test (χ²)

For categorical data — independence & goodness of fit

Definition

The Chi-Square (χ²) test is a non-parametric test used for categorical (nominal) data. It comes in two flavours:
(1) Test of Independence — determines whether two categorical variables are associated.
(2) Goodness of Fit — tests whether observed frequencies match expected frequencies from a theoretical distribution.

Key distinction from other tests: χ² works with counts (frequencies) in categories, not with continuous measurements or ranks.

Unlike the rank-based tests we've seen so far, Chi-Square doesn't rank anything. Instead, it compares how many people fall into each category against what we'd theoretically expect if there were no relationship. The bigger the gap between what we observe and what we expect, the larger the χ² statistic — and the stronger the evidence of an association.

💡 Intuition — "Expected vs. Observed"

Imagine you shuffle a deck of cards and deal them into two piles. You'd expect roughly equal numbers of red and black cards in each pile — not because you planned it, but because the two variables (which pile, what colour) are independent. If one pile ends up with 45 red cards and only 5 black, that's very surprising — evidence that something non-random happened.

Chi-Square formalises this intuition. Under independence, it calculates exactly how many cases we'd expect in each cell of a table. Then it measures how far the observed counts deviate from those expectations. Each cell contributes (O−E)²/E to the total — the squaring ensures deviations in either direction count positively, and dividing by E standardises for the scale of the expected count.

📚 Real Example: Vaccine Side-Effect by Age Group

Researchers record whether 200 individuals experienced a mild side-effect after vaccination, categorised by age group. Is the side-effect rate independent of age?

Side Effect: YesSide Effect: NoTotal
18–35 yrs3070100
36–60 yrs4555100
Total75125200
Expected(18-35, Yes) = (100 × 75) / 200 = 37.5
χ² = Σ [(O − E)² / E] = ... ≈ 4.27
df = (rows−1)(cols−1) = 1 × 1 = 1

✏ Worked Solution — Step by Step

Step 1 — State the Hypotheses

H₀: Vaccine side effects are independent of age group (no association).

H₁: Vaccine side effects are associated with age group.

Step 2 — Compute Expected Frequencies for Each Cell

Formula: E = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total

CellObserved (O)Row TotalCol TotalExpected (E)
18–35, Yes3010075(100×75)/200 = 37.5
18–35, No70100125(100×125)/200 = 62.5
36–60, Yes4510075(100×75)/200 = 37.5
36–60, No55100125(100×125)/200 = 62.5

All expected values ≥ 5 ✓ — the chi-square approximation is valid here.

Step 3 — Compute χ² = Σ [(O − E)² / E]

Cell 1: (30−37.5)² / 37.5 = (−7.5)² / 37.5 = 56.25 / 37.5 = 1.50

Cell 2: (70−62.5)² / 62.5 = (7.5)² / 62.5 = 56.25 / 62.5 = 0.90

Cell 3: (45−37.5)² / 37.5 = (7.5)² / 37.5 = 56.25 / 37.5 = 1.50

Cell 4: (55−62.5)² / 62.5 = (−7.5)² / 62.5 = 56.25 / 62.5 = 0.90

χ² = 1.50 + 0.90 + 1.50 + 0.90 = 4.80

Step 4 — Degrees of Freedom and Critical Value

df = (rows−1)(cols−1) = (2−1)(2−1) = 1

At α = 0.05 with df = 1, the critical χ² value = 3.841 (from chi-square table)

Our χ² = 4.80 > 3.841 → Reject H₀

The exact p-value ≈ 0.028, which is below 0.05 — confirming statistical significance.

✅ Conclusion

There is a statistically significant association between age group and vaccine side-effect rate (χ² = 4.80, df = 1, p = 0.028). The 36–60 age group experienced side effects at a higher rate (45%) compared to the 18–35 group (30%). Age is not independent of side-effect occurrence, and this difference is unlikely to be due to chance.

Formula

χ² = Σ [ (Oᵢ − Eᵢ)² / Eᵢ ]

Eᵢ = (Row Total × Column Total) / Grand Total
Oᵢ — Observed count in cell i
Eᵢ — Expected count if H₀ were true
df — (r−1)(c−1) where r=rows, c=cols
χ² — Compare to chi-square table at chosen α

⚠ Assumption

Each expected cell count must be ≥ 5. If not, use Fisher's Exact Test instead.

Stat:H = [12/N(N+1)]Σ[Rⱼ²/nⱼ] − 3(N+1)
Reject H₀:H > χ²(α, k−1)
Post-hoc:Mann-Whitney U + Bonferroni correction
Parametric ≡:One-way ANOVA
CORRELATION

Spearman's ρ

ρ
Use when:Ordinal data, outliers, non-linear monotonic trend
H₀:ρ = 0 (no monotonic association)
Stat:ρ = 1 − 6Σdᵢ² / n(n²−1)
Range:−1 to +1
|ρ| > 0.7:Strong correlation
Parametric ≡:Pearson's r
CATEGORICAL

Chi-Square Test (χ²)

χ²
Use when:Categorical variables; testing association or fit
H₀ (independence):The two variables are not associated
Stat:χ² = Σ[(O−E)²/E]
Expected:E = (Row Total × Col Total) / Grand Total
df:(rows−1)(cols−1)
Reject H₀:χ² > critical value from χ² table
Assumption:Expected count ≥ 5 in each cell
If fails:Use Fisher's Exact Test instead

Shared Assumptions of Rank-Based Tests

1
Independence of observations — Each data point must come from a different, unrelated subject (within groups).
2
Ordinal or continuous measurement scale — Data can be ranked; nominal data requires chi-square instead.
3
No specific distributional form — The distribution does not need to be normal, but groups should have similar shape (for comparison tests).
4
Random sampling — Data should be randomly sampled from the population of interest.
