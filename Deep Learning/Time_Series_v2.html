What is a Time Series?

A time series is any sequence of measurements recorded at successive, equally-spaced points in time. Stock prices, temperature readings, electricity consumption, heartbeats — if it has a timestamp and there's an ordering that matters, it's a time series.

The four components every time series has

Before you model anything, you need to understand what drives the data. Every time series can be decomposed into four parts:

📐 Additive Decomposition Model

Y(t) = Trend(t) + Seasonality(t) + Cycle(t) + Residual(t)

Trend — long-run direction (stock generally going up/down over years)
Seasonality — regular repeating pattern at fixed intervals (FMCGs see higher sales before Diwali, every year)
Cycle — irregular, longer-term waves (economic business cycles)
Residual (Noise) — what's left after removing the above — pure randomness

Stationarity — the single most important concept

A time series is stationary if its statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation) do not change over time. Most ML models implicitly assume stationarity — they can't distinguish "today's pattern" from "tomorrow's pattern" unless those patterns are consistent.

⚠ Why Raw Stock Prices Are Non-Stationary

A stock price of ₹100 in 2015 and ₹100 in 2024 mean completely different things relative to the company's size and the market environment. The mean and variance of raw prices drift over time — this breaks almost every model. Never train directly on raw prices without transformation.

Autocorrelation and Lag

Autocorrelation is the correlation of a series with a delayed copy of itself. If today's price is highly correlated with yesterday's price, that's autocorrelation at lag-1. This is what makes time series fundamentally different from tabular data — order matters and the past contains information about the future.

📐 Autocorrelation at Lag k
ACF(k) = Corr(Y_t, Y_{t-k})

If ACF(1) ≈ 0.95 → yesterday's price strongly predicts today's price
If ACF(7) ≈ 0.60 → 7 days ago still has predictive power (weekly seasonality)

What does "Look-back window" mean?

When we train a neural network, we feed it the last N time steps (the look-back window) and ask it to predict the next step (or next K steps). Choosing the right window size is crucial — too short and the model misses long-range dependencies; too long and it gets confused by irrelevant history.

Classical statistics gave us ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average) and its cousins. These work brilliantly when the assumptions hold. Neural networks come in when they don't.

Use these models for learning the craft and understanding what drives prices, not for blindly trading.

Theoretical Foundations of Time Series

Before writing a single line of PyTorch, you need a solid mental model of what's happening inside your data. This is not optional background reading. Every decision you will make — what window size to use, which model to pick, how to split your data, what loss function is appropriate — is rooted in these concepts. Skipping this section is what separates people who get a model to compile from people who understand why it works or fails.

We will cover: the four structural components of any time series, stationarity and why it governs everything, the full family of decomposition models, autocorrelation and its partial counterpart, classical forecasting models as a foundation for understanding deep learning, differencing and transformation, white noise and the random walk, forecasting horizons, endogenous vs. exogenous variables, and the critical rules of time series validation. Every concept will be illustrated with a live interactive chart you can inspect directly in this document.

1. The Four Structural Components

Any time series — stock price, rainfall, electricity demand, heartbeats — can be thought of as the sum (or product) of four underlying forces. Before you can model a series, you must recognise which of these forces are active in your data and how strongly each one dominates.

The Decomposition Model

Y(t) = Trend(t) + Seasonality(t) + Cycle(t) + Residual(t)

These four components are not just labels — they represent fundamentally different generating mechanisms. Understanding which one dominates your series tells you which type of model will work, which transformations you need, and what kind of forecast accuracy is even theoretically possible.

2. Trend — The Long-Run Direction

Trend is the slow, persistent movement of a series in one direction over a long period. It is the component that answers: "Is this series, overall, going up or down?" It is not the day-to-day noise or the regular seasonal wave — it is the underlying drift that persists across years.

Trends come in different mathematical flavours and recognising which type you have determines your transformation strategy:

Types of Trend
Linear Trend: T(t) = α + βt
— The series grows (or falls) by a constant absolute amount per period. GDP growing by a fixed dollar amount each year. Removable by first-order differencing: ΔY(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−1).

Exponential Trend: T(t) = α · e^(βt)
— The series grows by a constant percentage each period. Most financial assets and technology adoption curves. Removable by log-differencing: r(t) = log(Y(t)) − log(Y(t−1)).

Polynomial / Nonlinear Trend: T(t) = α + βt + γt²
— Acceleration or deceleration in growth. Removing this requires higher-order differencing or explicit modelling with a flexible nonlinear function (which is one reason neural networks are useful).

Stochastic Trend (Random Walk with Drift): Y(t) = δ + Y(t−1) + ε(t)
— The trend itself wanders randomly. Most stock prices exhibit this. The direction of "drift" (δ) is positive on average for equity markets, but the path is unpredictable. This is the hardest type because the trend is not a deterministic function of time — it evolves.
Trend is Not Signal — It is Structure

A common beginner mistake is to treat trend as something useful the model can exploit. In most cases, trend is a statistical property that must be removed before modelling because models cannot generalise across different absolute levels. A model trained on prices of ₹100–₹200 will have no idea what to do when prices reach ₹500. You remove trend through transformation, then add it back when interpreting results. The model learns the detrended dynamics.

Notice how the stochastic trend (random walk with drift) looks like a stock price — directional on average but unpredictable in path. The exponential trend's curvature becomes linear after taking logarithms.

3. Seasonality — Fixed-Period Repetition

Seasonality is any pattern that repeats at a fixed, known period. The word is misleading — it does not just mean spring/summer/autumn/winter. It means any regular rhythm: intra-day (every 24 hours), weekly (every 7 days), monthly (every 30 days), quarterly (every 90 days), or annual (every 365 days). The defining characteristic is that the period is fixed and known in advance.

A coffee shop gets busy from 8–9am every weekday — that is daily seasonality with a period of 24 hours. An FMCG company sells more during Diwali every year — that is annual seasonality. A stock might show lower volume every Friday afternoon because traders close positions before the weekend — that is weekly seasonality with period 5 (trading days).

Formal Definition
A time series Y(t) has seasonality of period s if:
E[Y(t)] ≈ E[Y(t − s)] for all t

Common periods in finance:
s = 5 → weekly (5 trading days)
s = 21 → monthly (~21 trading days)
s = 63 → quarterly (~63 trading days)
s = 252 → annual (~252 trading days)

Additive vs. Multiplicative Seasonality

The way seasonality interacts with trend determines which decomposition model you should use — this is one of the most practically important distinctions in time series analysis.

Additive Seasonality
Y(t) = T(t) + S(t) + R(t)
— The seasonal swing is a fixed absolute amount regardless of where the trend is. If a shop always sells exactly 200 extra units in December no matter its yearly volume, that is additive. The seasonal component S(t) is measured in the same units as Y.
Multiplicative Seasonality
Y(t) = T(t) × S(t) × R(t)
— The seasonal swing is a fixed percentage of the current level. If holiday sales are always 40% above baseline — and baseline is growing — the absolute swing grows too. The seasonal component S(t) is dimensionless (a multiplier). Taking log transforms converts this to additive: log(Y) = log(T) + log(S) + log(R).
How to Decide Which Model Applies

Plot the series. If the peaks and troughs look like they are getting further apart as the series grows — multiplicative. If they look like they maintain a constant height — additive. You can also check: if log-transforming makes the seasonal swings look equal in height, it was multiplicative. Financial returns tend to be multiplicative (use log-returns). Demand with a flat baseline tends to be additive.

Interactive — Additive vs. Multiplicative Seasonality

The additive series (teal) has constant-height seasonal swings. The multiplicative series (orange) has swings that grow with the trend — common in financial data.

4. Cycle — Irregular Long-Term Waves

Cycle is the component that most beginners conflate with seasonality — and getting this distinction right is important. A cycle is a pattern that rises and falls, but at irregular intervals and over longer time frames than seasonality. The period is not fixed and not known in advance.

The business cycle is the canonical example: economies expand for a few years, peak, contract in a recession, trough, and recover — but no two business cycles are the same length. The technology adoption cycle (hype → correction → plateau) is another. In financial markets, credit cycles, commodity super-cycles, and bull/bear market cycles all exhibit this character: persistent multi-year trends that eventually reverse, without a fixed calendar period.

Cycle vs. Seasonality — The Key Distinction

Seasonality: Fixed period. You know it will happen every 12 months, every 7 days, every 24 hours. It is deterministic in its timing. You can model it with dummy variables, Fourier terms, or periodic embeddings.

Cycle: Variable period. You do not know when the next recession will start, how long the bull market will last, or when a commodity cycle will turn. It is stochastic in its timing. You cannot model it with a calendar — you have to detect regime changes in the data itself.

In practice, cycles are the hardest component to model. Classical ARIMA largely ignores them. Neural networks, especially LSTMs and Transformers with long context windows, are better positioned to detect cyclical patterns — but only if the training data spans multiple complete cycles.

Cycles and Training Data Length

A model trained on 2 years of stock data has never seen a full business cycle (which typically spans 5–10 years). It cannot learn anything about cyclical behaviour because it has not observed enough complete cycles. This is why financial models built on short training windows often fail catastrophically during recessions — the model has literally never seen a contraction. For cyclical modelling, you need data spanning multiple cycles, which often means decades.

Interactive — Cycle Component (Irregular Period) vs. Seasonality (Fixed Period)

Teal bars mark seasonal peaks — they are evenly spaced. Orange markers show cyclical peaks — irregular spacing, variable amplitude. This is the difference between something you can schedule and something you have to detect.

5. Residual (Noise) — What Remains

The residual — also called the irregular component or noise — is everything left in the data after the trend, seasonality, and cycle have been accounted for. In a well-specified model, the residual should be pure white noise: random, structureless, and unpredictable.

White Noise
ε(t) ~ WN(0, σ²)
Conditions: E[ε(t)] = 0, Var[ε(t)] = σ² (constant), Cov[ε(t), ε(t−k)] = 0 for k ≠ 0

Zero mean. Constant variance. Zero autocorrelation at every lag. Any violation of these conditions means there is still exploitable structure in your residuals — and your model is incomplete.

Checking that your model's residuals are white noise is one of the most important diagnostics in time series analysis. If the residuals still show autocorrelation, heteroskedasticity (changing variance), or seasonal patterns, the model has failed to extract all available signal. The residual check — via an ACF plot of residuals and a Ljung-Box test — is not optional; it is how you know whether your model is finished or still broken.

6. Stationarity — The Governing Concept

Stationarity is the single most important property in time series analysis. A series is weakly stationary (also called covariance stationary) if three conditions hold for all time t and all lags k:

Conditions for Weak Stationarity
1. E[Y(t)] = μ (constant mean — does not drift)
2. Var[Y(t)] = σ² (constant variance — does not explode or shrink)
3. Cov[Y(t), Y(t−k)] = γ(k) (autocovariance depends only on lag k, not on t itself)

If all three hold, the series "looks the same" statistically at any point in time. A pattern learned from 2018 data still applies in 2024. This is the property that makes a trained model generalisable.

Most real-world series are non-stationary. Raw stock prices violate all three conditions: the mean drifts upward with time, the variance (volatility) changes with market regimes, and the covariance structure changes as market conditions evolve. This is why you almost never train a neural network directly on raw prices.

Interactive — Stationary vs. Non-Stationary Series

The teal series fluctuates around a constant mean with constant variance — stationary. The orange series drifts and widens over time — non-stationary. A model trained on one stretch of the orange series will not generalise to another stretch.

Testing for Stationarity

You do not just visually inspect — you use statistical tests. The two most common are the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test and the KPSS test. They have opposite null hypotheses, so you use them together:

ADF + KPSS Together

ADF Test: Null hypothesis = series has a unit root (non-stationary). If p-value < 0.05, reject the null — series is likely stationary.

KPSS Test: Null hypothesis = series is stationary. If p-value < 0.05, reject the null — series is likely non-stationary.

Both agreeing is the clearest signal. If ADF says stationary but KPSS disagrees (or vice versa), you have a borderline case requiring deeper investigation. In Python: statsmodels.tsa.stattools.adfuller() and statsmodels.tsa.stattools.kpss().

7. Differencing — Making a Series Stationary

Differencing is the primary tool for removing trend (and stochastic non-stationarity) from a series. Instead of modelling Y(t) directly, you model the change from one period to the next. This eliminates the drift and anchors the series around zero.

Orders of Differencing
First-order differencing:
ΔY(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−1)
Removes a linear trend. For stock prices, this gives you raw daily price changes (in currency units).

Log-differencing (log-returns):
r(t) = log(Y(t)) − log(Y(t−1)) ≈ (Y(t) − Y(t−1)) / Y(t−1)
Removes an exponential trend and gives continuously compounded returns. More symmetric, better-behaved statistically, and scale-invariant across assets with different price levels. This is the standard transformation for financial time series.

Second-order differencing:
Δ²Y(t) = ΔY(t) − ΔY(t−1)
Removes a quadratic trend. Rarely needed in finance, more common in economic series with accelerating growth.

Seasonal differencing:
ΔₛY(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−s)
Removes a seasonal pattern of period s. For weekly seasonality in stock data: Δ₅Y(t) = Y(t) − Y(t−5). Often combined with first-order differencing in SARIMA models.

Interactive — Raw Price Series vs. Log-Returns (After Differencing)

The raw price (orange) drifts with non-constant variance. The log-returns (teal) oscillate around zero with approximately constant variance — stationary. Notice how the high-volatility regime around step 50 appears as larger returns, not as a level change.

Inverse Transformation — Do Not Forget This

When you train on differenced or log-return data, your model outputs returns, not prices. To recover a price prediction, you must invert the transformation: Predicted_Price(t) = exp(r̂(t)) × Actual_Price(t−1). Many beginners forget this step and then cannot understand why their "predictions" look nothing like prices. Build the inverse transform into your inference pipeline from day one.

8. Autocorrelation (ACF) and Partial Autocorrelation (PACF)

Autocorrelation measures the correlation between a series and a delayed (lagged) copy of itself. It is the quantitative answer to: "Does knowing what the series did k periods ago help predict what it does now?" This is what makes time series fundamentally different from tabular data — the ordering of observations contains information.

Autocorrelation Function (ACF)
ACF(k) = Cov[Y(t), Y(t−k)] / Var[Y(t)] = Corr(Y_t, Y_{t−k})

ACF(0) = 1 always (a series is perfectly correlated with itself at lag 0)
ACF(1) ≈ 0.95 → yesterday's value strongly predicts today's
ACF(7) ≈ 0.60 → 7 days ago still has predictive power (weekly pattern)
ACF(k) ≈ 0 for all k > 0 → white noise, nothing is predictable

The problem with ACF alone is that if lag-1 is correlated, lag-2 will appear correlated simply because of the chain through lag-1 — not because lag-2 has any direct relationship with the present. Partial Autocorrelation (PACF) strips out these indirect, chained effects. PACF(k) measures the correlation between Y(t) and Y(t−k) after statistically removing the influence of all lags in between (lags 1 through k−1).

ACF vs. PACF — Practical Decision Rules

ACF cuts off sharply after lag q, PACF decays slowly: Suggests a Moving Average process of order q — MA(q). The shock from q periods ago still directly affects the present, but shocks beyond q do not.

PACF cuts off sharply after lag p, ACF decays slowly: Suggests an Autoregressive process of order p — AR(p). Only the last p values directly drive the present.

Both ACF and PACF decay slowly: Suggests ARIMA or nonlinear dependencies. This is the typical signature of financial returns — and one reason neural networks are compelling here. They can model the nonlinear autocorrelation structure that ARIMA cannot.

For deep learning: PACF gives you a data-driven starting point for your look-back window. If PACF drops to near-zero after lag 15, feeding the model 60 past steps probably adds mostly noise. Start with a window equal to where PACF becomes insignificant, then tune from there.

9. Classical Forecasting Models — The Foundation

You do not need to implement these models from scratch — Python's statsmodels library handles them. But understanding them is essential because they formalise the theory from above into explicit equations, and deep learning models are often best understood as nonlinear generalisations of these classical approaches.

AR(p) — AutoRegressive Model
Y(t) = c + φ₁Y(t−1) + φ₂Y(t−2) + ... + φₚY(t−p) + ε(t)

The present value is a weighted sum of the last p values. Coefficients φᵢ are learned by minimising squared error. PACF cuts off at lag p. A vanilla RNN is essentially a nonlinear AR model with learnable, nonlinear weights.
MA(q) — Moving Average Model
Y(t) = μ + ε(t) + θ₁ε(t−1) + θ₂ε(t−2) + ... + θqε(t−q)

The present value depends on the current shock and the last q shocks (past prediction errors). Not the same as a moving average filter. ACF cuts off at lag q. Captures how shocks propagate forward in time.
ARIMA(p, d, q) — Combining All Three
Apply d rounds of differencing to make the series stationary, then fit AR(p) + MA(q).

d = 0: series is already stationary. d = 1: one round of differencing. d = 2: two rounds (rare). p controls how many past values enter directly. q controls how many past errors enter directly.
SARIMA(p, d, q)(P, D, Q)[s] — Adding Seasonal Terms
Extends ARIMA with seasonal autoregressive (P), differencing (D), and moving average (Q) components at period s.

Example: SARIMA(1,1,1)(1,1,1)[12] for monthly data — handles both short-run dynamics and annual seasonality. Understanding this is what makes the LSTM's sequence-to-sequence structure intuitive: LSTMs are doing something analogous, but with learned nonlinear weights instead of fixed linear coefficients.

10. The Random Walk — Setting a Baseline

The random walk is the simplest possible stochastic process and serves as the baseline forecast against which all models must be compared. If your model cannot beat a random walk, it has learned nothing actionable.

Random Walk
Y(t) = Y(t−1) + ε(t), ε(t) ~ N(0, σ²)

Best forecast: Ŷ(t+1) = Y(t) (predict "no change")
Mean Squared Error of this naive forecast: σ²

A pure random walk has no memory — each step is a fresh shock. The "no change" forecast is the theoretically optimal forecast under this model. If your LSTM's MSE is larger than σ², the LSTM is doing worse than guessing. This happens more often than beginners expect in financial data.
Random Walk with Drift
Y(t) = δ + Y(t−1) + ε(t)

δ is the drift term — positive for equity markets over long horizons (equities generally go up). This is how stock indices behave: a long-run upward drift (δ > 0) plus random noise. The naive forecast becomes Ŷ(t+1) = Y(t) + δ̂, where δ̂ is the estimated average daily return from training data.

Interactive — Three Simulated Random Walk Paths from the Same Starting Point

All three paths start from the same point but diverge completely. They look like stock price charts because most stock prices are close to random walks. The drift (positive on average) is subtle. Your model must extract more information than this — otherwise, use the naive baseline.

11. Forecasting Horizon and Strategy

Every forecasting problem has a horizon — how many steps ahead you are predicting. The horizon fundamentally changes which architecture is appropriate and how errors accumulate.

The Four Forecasting Strategies

One-step-ahead (h = 1): Predict the very next time step. Simplest case. Model outputs a single scalar. Most research benchmarks use this because it is the cleanest comparison. Accuracy degrades as you move to longer horizons.

Direct multi-step (h = k, separate models): Train one model for each horizon — Model₁ predicts day+1, Model₂ predicts day+2, ..., Modelₖ predicts day+k. Each model is optimised for its specific horizon. Clean but expensive: k times the training cost.

Recursive multi-step (one model, feedback): Predict t+1, feed that prediction back as input, predict t+2, and so on. Errors compound — the model's own mistakes contaminate subsequent predictions. Cheap but fragile over long horizons. This is what you do naturally with an LSTM's hidden state.

Sequence-to-sequence (one model, direct multi-output): The model ingests a sequence of N steps and produces a sequence of k future steps in a single forward pass, without recursive feedback. LSTMs with a decoder, and Transformers, do this naturally. This is the gold standard for medium-horizon forecasting.

12. Endogenous vs. Exogenous Variables

When you build a multivariate time series model, you need to know which variables are which — because they have different rules about how they can be used.

Variable Classification

Endogenous (Y): The variable you are predicting. Its future values are unknown at prediction time — you cannot feed them as input. You can only use its past values (lags). For stock price forecasting, the closing price is endogenous.

Exogenous (X): External variables that help explain Y but are not themselves being predicted. If you know their future values at prediction time, you can feed them directly. If not, you must lag them. Examples: trading volume, RSI, VIX, interest rates, earnings announcements, sentiment scores. These are what make multivariate models useful — they bring in information the price series alone does not contain.

Lagged exogenous: Exogenous variables shifted back in time so that at prediction time, you only use values that are already known. If you want to use today's volume to predict tomorrow's price, that is a lagged exogenous variable — and it is perfectly legitimate. In PyTorch, these simply become additional columns in your input tensor.

13. Temporal Leakage and the Rules of Validation

This is where the largest percentage of real-world time series projects silently fail. Temporal leakage occurs when information from the future contaminates the model's training or feature construction, producing metrics that are optimistic during development and catastrophic in production.

Never Shuffle Time Series Data

If you randomly shuffle time series data before splitting into train/test (as you would for image classification), you will train on future observations and evaluate on past ones. This is not just a methodological error — it is guaranteed to give you misleadingly good metrics. The model appears to predict the future, but it has already seen it during training. Your reported accuracy will have zero relationship with real-world performance.

Always split chronologically: train on the past, validate on more recent data, test on the most recent data. No exceptions.

Interactive — Correct Chronological Train / Validation / Test Split

Time flows left to right. Training (teal) always comes first, validation (orange) in the middle, test (purple) last. The test set represents the deployment scenario: the model has never touched these observations. This is the only valid way to evaluate a time series model.

Beyond a simple chronological split, walk-forward (rolling origin) validation is the gold standard for time series. You train on [0, t], test on [t+1, t+h], then expand the window to [0, t+h], test on [t+h+1, t+2h], and repeat. This simulates live deployment where the model is periodically retrained. It gives a more realistic distribution of errors across different market regimes — particularly important for financial data where volatility regimes change.

14. Volatility Clustering and Heteroskedasticity

Financial time series have one more property that is almost universally present and frequently ignored: volatility clustering. Periods of high volatility tend to be followed by more high volatility, and calm periods tend to persist. This is called conditional heteroskedasticity — the variance of the residuals is not constant; it depends on recent history.

ARCH Effect (AutoRegressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity)
ε(t) = σ(t) · z(t), z(t) ~ N(0,1)
σ²(t) = ω + α₁ε²(t−1) + α₂ε²(t−2) + ...

The variance σ²(t) at time t depends on how large the recent shocks were. A big shock yesterday makes tomorrow's variance larger. This is why financial returns show quiet patches and stormy patches — the variance has memory even when the returns themselves do not.

For deep learning, this matters in two ways: first, your model's loss function assumes i.i.d. errors, but heteroskedastic residuals violate this assumption. Second, and more importantly, LSTMs can learn volatility regimes implicitly if trained carefully — this is one of the places where deep learning genuinely outperforms classical linear models, which require GARCH-family models to handle this explicitly.

Interactive — Volatility Clustering in Returns (Large Shocks Cluster Together)

Notice how large swings (both positive and negative) tend to appear together in time, and quiet periods also cluster. This is the ARCH effect. An LSTM trained on sufficiently long sequences can learn to detect when the series is entering a high-volatility regime and adjust its predictions accordingly.

Summary — What to Apply Before You Model

1. Decompose — identify trend, seasonality, cycle, and residual in your series visually and statistically.
2. Test stationarity — ADF + KPSS. If non-stationary, apply the appropriate transformation (log-returns for finance).
3. Plot ACF and PACF on the transformed series — use them to choose your look-back window.
4. Check for volatility clustering — plot absolute returns or squared returns ACF. Persistent autocorrelation in squared returns = ARCH effect present.
5. Choose your horizon and strategy — one-step vs. multi-step, recursive vs. direct vs. seq2seq.
6. Split chronologically — never shuffle. Consider walk-forward validation for final evaluation.
7. Establish a naive baseline — the random walk "predict last value" forecast. Your model must beat this to be useful.

These are not abstract academic concepts. Every single one of them maps directly onto a decision you will make when building the PyTorch pipelines in the sections that follow. The window size comes from PACF. The input transformation comes from stationarity analysis. The train/test split strategy comes from temporal leakage rules. The baseline comes from the random walk. The choice between additive and multiplicative decomposition determines whether you work in price space or log-return space.

Exploratory Analysis

Never jump straight to modelling. A quick EDA tells you what transformations you need, what patterns exist, and whether your data makes sense at all.

Log Returns — the correct unit of analysis

Instead of raw prices, we compute log returns. Here's why this is the standard in finance:

📐 Log Return
r_t = ln(P_t / P_{t-1}) = ln(P_t) − ln(P_{t-1})

Why log?
1. Additive over time: r_1 + r_2 + ... + r_n = total log return
2. Approximately normal (easier for models)
3. Stationary (the mean and variance are stable)
4. Comparable across assets (₹ vs $ vs BTC doesn't matter)

Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test

The ADF test is a statistical test for stationarity. The null hypothesis is "the series has a unit root" (i.e., it is non-stationary). A p-value < 0.05 means we reject that null — the series is stationary.

Preprocessing Pipeline

This is the most important section for real-world work. A bad pipeline guarantees a bad model. We'll build a reusable, leakage-free pipeline that can be serialized and used in production.

Step 1 — Feature Engineering

We'll use each asset as a feature column (multivariate input). This lets the model learn cross-asset relationships — e.g., when AAPL drops, does BTC follow? We'll also add technical features.

Step 2 — Train / Validation / Test Split (Time-Aware)

⚠ Critical: Never Shuffle Time Series Data

In normal tabular ML, we shuffle and split randomly. In time series, shuffling causes data leakage — your model sees future data during training. Always split chronologically: the first 70% for training, next 15% for validation, last 15% for testing.

Train (70%)
2021-01 → 2023-02
→
Validation (15%)
2023-02 → 2023-07
→
Test (15%)
2023-07 → 2024-01

Step 3 — Scaling

We fit the StandardScaler only on the training set, then apply it to validation and test. Fitting on the full dataset leaks future information about the mean and variance — another form of data leakage.

Step 4 — Sliding Window Sequence Creation

This converts our 2D DataFrame (rows × features) into 3D tensors (samples × time_steps × features) that neural networks expect.

📐 Sliding Window
Window size W = 60 (days), forecast horizon H = 1 (day)

Sample 0: X = [day_0 ... day_59], y = [day_60]
Sample 1: X = [day_1 ... day_60], y = [day_61]
Sample 2: X = [day_2 ... day_61], y = [day_62]

def make_sequences(X: np.ndarray, window: int, target_col: int, horizon: int = 1):
    """
    Converts a 2D array (time × features) into supervised learning sequences.

    Args:
        X          : scaled feature array, shape (T, F)
        window     : number of past time steps to use as input
        target_col : column index of the feature we want to predict
        horizon    : how many steps ahead to predict (default=1)

    Returns:
        xs : np.ndarray of shape (N, window, F)  — input sequences
        ys : np.ndarray of shape (N,)             — targets (scalar, next return)
    """
    xs, ys = [], []
    for i in range(len(X) - window - horizon + 1):
        xs.append(X[i : i + window])                  # (window, F)
        ys.append(X[i + window + horizon - 1, target_col])  # scalar
    return np.array(xs, dtype=np.float32), np.array(ys, dtype=np.float32)

PyTorch Dataset & DataLoader

PyTorch's Dataset and DataLoader handle batching, shuffling (only within-train!), and feeding data to the GPU efficiently. We create one reusable class that works for all our models.

Why Batching Matters

Training on one sample at a time is slow. Training on the entire dataset at once uses too much memory and finds poor optima. Mini-batches (32–256 samples) strike the right balance — they compute a noisy but directionally correct gradient, which actually helps escape local minima.

Architecture 1 — Vanilla RNN

The Recurrent Neural Network is the simplest sequential model. It processes the sequence one step at a time, maintaining a hidden state — a vector that summarises everything seen so far.

📐 RNN Update Equation
h_t = tanh(W_h · h_{t-1} + W_x · x_t + b)

h_t = hidden state at time t | x_t = input at time t | T = last time step

The Vanishing Gradient Problem

Because gradients are multiplied through each time step during backpropagation, they either shrink to zero (vanish) or explode over long sequences. With 60 time steps, the gradient signal from step 60 barely reaches step 1 — the model can't learn long-range dependencies.

⚠ When to use Vanilla RNN

Only for short sequences (< 20 steps) or educational purposes. In practice, always prefer LSTM or GRU. We implement it here to understand the why behind LSTM's existence.

import torch.nn as nn

class VanillaRNN(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, hidden_size=64, num_layers=2,
                 dropout=0.2, output_size=1):
        super().__init__()
        self.hidden_size = hidden_size
        self.num_layers  = num_layers

        # nn.RNN(input, hidden, layers, batch_first, dropout)
        # batch_first=True → input shape: (batch, seq, features)
        self.rnn = nn.RNN(
            input_size  = input_size,
            hidden_size = hidden_size,
            num_layers  = num_layers,
            batch_first = True,
            dropout     = dropout if num_layers > 1 else 0,
            nonlinearity= 'tanh'
        )
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        self.fc      = nn.Linear(hidden_size, output_size)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: (batch, seq_len, input_size)
        out, _ = self.rnn(x)     # out: (batch, seq_len, hidden_size)
        out = out[:, -1, :]      # take only the LAST time step's hidden state
        out = self.dropout(out)
        return self.fc(out).squeeze(-1)  # (batch,)

Architecture 2 — LSTM

The Long Short-Term Memory network (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) was designed specifically to solve the vanishing gradient problem. It does this by adding a cell state — a separate "conveyor belt" of information that can flow through many time steps with minimal modification.

The Three Gates

The LSTM controls information flow using three sigmoid gates (each outputs a value between 0 and 1, where 0 = "block everything" and 1 = "let everything through"):

📐 LSTM Equations
f_t = σ(W_f · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_f) ← Forget Gate: what to erase from cell state
i_t = σ(W_i · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_i) ← Input Gate: what new info to write
g_t = tanh(W_g · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_g) ← Candidate: the actual new content
o_t = σ(W_o · [h_{t-1}, x_t] + b_o) ← Output Gate: what to expose as h_t

C_t = f_t ⊙ C_{t-1} + i_t ⊙ g_t ← update cell state
h_t = o_t ⊙ tanh(C_t) ← new hidden state

The cell state C_t can carry information across hundreds of time steps without gradient vanishing — that's the key insight. The LSTM literally learns what to remember, what to forget, and what to output.

code 
class LSTMModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, hidden_size=128, num_layers=2,
                 dropout=0.3, output_size=1):
        super().__init__()
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(
            input_size  = input_size,
            hidden_size = hidden_size,
            num_layers  = num_layers,
            batch_first = True,
            dropout     = dropout if num_layers > 1 else 0,
            bidirectional = False,  # set True to see past AND future context
        )
        # Layer norm stabilises training (especially for financial data)
        self.norm    = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_size)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        # Two-layer projection head
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_size, hidden_size // 2),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout / 2),
            nn.Linear(hidden_size // 2, output_size),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: (batch, seq_len, input_size)
        lstm_out, (h_n, c_n) = self.lstm(x)
        # Use the last hidden state of the last layer
        last_hidden = lstm_out[:, -1, :]    # (batch, hidden_size)
        last_hidden = self.norm(last_hidden)
        last_hidden = self.dropout(last_hidden)
        return self.fc(last_hidden).squeeze(-1)  # (batch,)

Architecture 3 — GRU

The Gated Recurrent Unit (Cho et al., 2014) is a simplified LSTM. It merges the forget and input gates into a single update gate, and eliminates the separate cell state. The result is ~33% fewer parameters with similar performance on most tasks.

📐 GRU Equations
z_t = σ(W_z · [h_{t-1}, x_t]) ← Update Gate: blend of forget + input
r_t = σ(W_r · [h_{t-1}, x_t]) ← Reset Gate: how much past to forget
ñ_t = tanh(W · [r_t ⊙ h_{t-1}, x_t]) ← Candidate hidden state
h_t = (1 − z_t) ⊙ h_{t-1} + z_t ⊙ ñ_t ← New hidden state
💡 LSTM vs GRU — Which to use?

GRU trains faster with less data. LSTM can theoretically store more complex long-range patterns. In practice for financial data (~1000 rows), GRU usually matches or beats LSTM because overfitting is the bigger risk. Try both and validate.

code 
class GRUModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, hidden_size=128, num_layers=2,
                 dropout=0.3, output_size=1):
        super().__init__()
        self.gru = nn.GRU(
            input_size  = input_size,
            hidden_size = hidden_size,
            num_layers  = num_layers,
            batch_first = True,
            dropout     = dropout if num_layers > 1 else 0,
        )
        self.norm    = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_size)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_size, hidden_size // 2),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout / 2),
            nn.Linear(hidden_size // 2, output_size),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        out, _ = self.gru(x)
        last   = self.norm(out[:, -1, :])
        return self.fc(self.dropout(last)).squeeze(-1)

Architecture 4 — Temporal CNN (TCN)

The Temporal Convolutional Network uses 1D convolutions with two key tricks: causal padding (only looks at past, never future) and dilation (exponentially growing receptive field). Unlike RNNs, all time steps are processed in parallel — no sequential bottleneck.

Dilated Causal Convolutions

Dilation means skipping input positions with exponentially growing gaps. With dilation rate d=1,2,4,8 and kernel size k=3, you can cover 2^4 × (3-1) = 32 time steps with just 4 layers. This gives the TCN a very large receptive field cheaply.

📐 Dilated Convolution Receptive Field
Receptive field = 1 + (kernel_size − 1) × Σ dilation_rates
With kernel=3, dilations=[1,2,4,8,16]:
= 1 + 2×(1+2+4+8+16) = 1 + 62 = 63 time steps covered
💡 Why TCN can be better than LSTM for long sequences

No hidden state means no sequential dependency → fully parallel training. Gradient path length is O(log N) not O(N). Stable training with no vanishing gradients. Often 3-5× faster to train than LSTM with similar accuracy.

class CausalConv1d(nn.Module):
    """1D conv that only looks at past (causal), with dilation support."""
    def __init__(self, in_ch, out_ch, kernel_size, dilation):
        super().__init__()
        self.pad  = (kernel_size - 1) * dilation  # left padding only → causal
        self.conv = nn.Conv1d(in_ch, out_ch, kernel_size,
                              dilation=dilation, padding=0)

    def forward(self, x):
        x = nn.functional.pad(x, (self.pad, 0))  # pad left only
        return self.conv(x)


class TCNBlock(nn.Module):
    """Residual dilated causal conv block."""
    def __init__(self, n_ch, kernel_size, dilation, dropout=0.2):
        super().__init__()
        self.block = nn.Sequential(
            CausalConv1d(n_ch, n_ch, kernel_size, dilation),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(n_ch),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
            CausalConv1d(n_ch, n_ch, kernel_size, dilation),
            nn.BatchNorm1d(n_ch),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Dropout(dropout),
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        return x + self.block(x)   # residual connection


class TCNModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, n_channels=64, kernel_size=3,
                 dilations=(1,2,4,8,16), dropout=0.2, output_size=1):
        super().__init__()
        # Project input features → channel dimension
        self.input_proj = nn.Conv1d(input_size, n_channels, kernel_size=1)
        # Stack of dilated causal conv blocks
        self.blocks = nn.ModuleList([
            TCNBlock(n_channels, kernel_size, d, dropout)
            for d in dilations
        ])
        self.fc = nn.Linear(n_channels, output_size)

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: (batch, seq_len, features) → transpose for Conv1d: (batch, features, seq_len)
        x = x.transpose(1, 2)
        x = self.input_proj(x)
        for block in self.blocks:
            x = block(x)
        x = x[:, :, -1]           # last time step only
        return self.fc(x).squeeze(-1)

Architecture 5 — Transformer

The Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) uses self-attention instead of recurrence. Each position in the sequence attends to all other positions simultaneously, computing a weighted sum of their values. This gives global context in a single layer.

Self-Attention

📐 Scaled Dot-Product Attention
Attention(Q, K, V) = softmax(Q · Kᵀ / √d_k) · V

Q = Query (what we're looking for)
K = Key (what each position offers)
V = Value (the actual content to retrieve)
d_k = key dimension (for scaling — prevents gradient vanishing in softmax)

Positional Encoding

Transformers process all positions in parallel — they have no built-in sense of time order. We must inject positional information explicitly via sinusoidal encodings added to the input embeddings.

📐 Sinusoidal Positional Encoding
PE(pos, 2i) = sin(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))
PE(pos, 2i+1) = cos(pos / 10000^(2i/d_model))
code 
import math

class PositionalEncoding(nn.Module):
    """Injects time-position information into the input embeddings."""
    def __init__(self, d_model, max_len=512, dropout=0.1):
        super().__init__()
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        pe = torch.zeros(max_len, d_model)
        pos = torch.arange(0, max_len, dtype=torch.float).unsqueeze(1)
        div = torch.exp(torch.arange(0, d_model, 2, dtype=torch.float)
                        * (-math.log(10000.0) / d_model))
        pe[:, 0::2] = torch.sin(pos * div)
        pe[:, 1::2] = torch.cos(pos * div)
        self.register_buffer('pe', pe.unsqueeze(0))  # (1, max_len, d_model)

    def forward(self, x):
        return self.dropout(x + self.pe[:, :x.size(1)])


class TransformerModel(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, input_size, d_model=64, nhead=4, num_layers=2,
                 dim_feedforward=256, dropout=0.1, output_size=1):
        super().__init__()
        # Project raw features → d_model dimensional space
        self.input_proj = nn.Linear(input_size, d_model)
        self.pos_enc    = PositionalEncoding(d_model, dropout=dropout)

        # Standard PyTorch TransformerEncoder
        enc_layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model        = d_model,
            nhead          = nhead,
            dim_feedforward= dim_feedforward,
            dropout        = dropout,
            batch_first    = True,
            norm_first     = True,   # pre-norm (more stable training)
        )
        self.transformer = nn.TransformerEncoder(enc_layer, num_layers=num_layers)

        # Causal mask: each position only attends to past positions
        self.register_buffer('causal_mask', None)

        self.norm = nn.LayerNorm(d_model)
        self.fc   = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(d_model, d_model // 2),
            nn.GELU(),
            nn.Linear(d_model // 2, output_size),
        )

    def _get_mask(self, seq_len, device):
        """Upper-triangular causal mask (future positions → -inf)."""
        mask = torch.triu(
            torch.full((seq_len, seq_len), float('-inf'), device=device), diagonal=1
        )
        return mask

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: (batch, seq_len, input_size)
        x    = self.input_proj(x)          # (batch, seq_len, d_model)
        x    = self.pos_enc(x)
        mask = self._get_mask(x.size(1), x.device)
        x    = self.transformer(x, mask=mask, is_causal=True)
        x    = self.norm(x[:, -1, :])     # last time step
        return self.fc(x).squeeze(-1)

The Training Loop

Every PyTorch training loop follows the same pattern. We add three important extras: learning rate scheduling, gradient clipping (critical for RNN/LSTM stability), and early stopping.

What each component does

Loss Function — MSELoss for regression. Measures the squared difference between predicted and actual returns. We also track MAE (less sensitive to outliers).

Optimizer — AdamW with weight decay. Adam adapts the learning rate per parameter. Weight decay adds L2 regularisation to prevent overfitting.

Scheduler — ReduceLROnPlateau. If validation loss stops improving, divide the learning rate by a factor. This lets the model take bigger steps early and fine-tune later.

Gradient Clipping — Caps gradient norm at 1.0. Prevents exploding gradients (especially important for RNN/LSTM on volatile financial data).

Early Stopping — Stop training when validation loss hasn't improved for patience epochs. Saves the best model checkpoint automatically.

code 
import copy
from torch.optim.lr_scheduler import ReduceLROnPlateau

def train_model(
    model,
    train_loader,
    val_loader,
    epochs        = 100,
    lr            = 1e-3,
    weight_decay  = 1e-4,
    patience      = 15,
    grad_clip     = 1.0,
    model_name    = "model",
    verbose       = True,
):
    """
    Universal training loop for all time series models.

    Returns:
        best_model : copy of model at best validation loss
        history    : dict with train_loss, val_loss lists
    """
    criterion = nn.MSELoss()
    optimizer = torch.optim.AdamW(model.parameters(), lr=lr, weight_decay=weight_decay)
    scheduler = ReduceLROnPlateau(optimizer, mode='min', factor=0.5,
                                    patience=7, verbose=False)

    history    = {'train_loss': [], 'val_loss': []}
    best_loss  = float('inf')
    best_state = None
    patience_counter = 0

    for epoch in range(1, epochs + 1):

        # ── Training phase ────────────────────────────────────────
        model.train()
        train_loss = 0.0
        for xb, yb in train_loader:
            xb, yb = xb.to(DEVICE), yb.to(DEVICE)
            optimizer.zero_grad()         # clear old gradients
            preds = model(xb)               # forward pass
            loss  = criterion(preds, yb)    # compute loss
            loss.backward()               # backpropagation
            nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(model.parameters(), grad_clip)
            optimizer.step()              # update weights
            train_loss += loss.item() * len(xb)
        train_loss /= len(train_loader.dataset)

        # ── Validation phase (no gradients needed) ────────────────
        model.eval()
        val_loss = 0.0
        with torch.no_grad():
            for xb, yb in val_loader:
                xb, yb = xb.to(DEVICE), yb.to(DEVICE)
                preds    = model(xb)
                val_loss += criterion(preds, yb).item() * len(xb)
        val_loss /= len(val_loader.dataset)

        history['train_loss'].append(train_loss)
        history['val_loss'].append(val_loss)
        scheduler.step(val_loss)

        # ── Early stopping + checkpoint ───────────────────────────
        if val_loss < best_loss:
            best_loss  = val_loss
            best_state = copy.deepcopy(model.state_dict())
            patience_counter = 0
            torch.save(best_state, f"{model_name}_best.pt")
        else:
            patience_counter += 1

        if verbose and epoch % 10 == 0:
            lr_now = optimizer.param_groups[0]['lr']
            print(f"[{model_name}] Epoch {epoch:3d}/{epochs} | "
                  f"Train: {train_loss:.6f} | Val: {val_loss:.6f} | "
                  f"LR: {lr_now:.6f} | Patience: {patience_counter}/{patience}")

        if patience_counter >= patience:
            print(f"[{model_name}] Early stop at epoch {epoch}. Best val loss: {best_loss:.6f}")
            break

    # Restore best weights
    model.load_state_dict(best_state)
    return model, history

Evaluation Metrics

MSE alone tells you very little. For time series, you need a suite of metrics — and for financial applications, directional accuracy (did we predict the sign correctly?) is often more actionable than magnitude error.

📐 Metrics Glossary
MAE = (1/n) Σ |ŷ_i − y_i| ← interpretable, in same units
RMSE = √[(1/n) Σ (ŷ_i − y_i)²] ← penalises large errors more
MAPE = (100/n) Σ |ŷ_i − y_i| / |y_i| ← % error (careful: bad if y≈0)
DA = (1/n) Σ 𝟙[sign(ŷ_i) == sign(y_i)] ← directional accuracy (0.5 = random)
code 
def evaluate_model(model, loader, model_name=""):
    """Run inference on a DataLoader and compute all evaluation metrics."""
    model.eval()
    preds_all, true_all = [], []

    with torch.no_grad():
        for xb, yb in loader:
            preds = model(xb.to(DEVICE)).cpu().numpy()
            preds_all.extend(preds)
            true_all.extend(yb.numpy())

    y_pred = np.array(preds_all)
    y_true = np.array(true_all)

    mae  = np.mean(np.abs(y_pred - y_true))
    rmse = np.sqrt(np.mean((y_pred - y_true)**2))
    # MAPE: skip near-zero true values (log-returns near 0 blow up MAPE)
    nz   = np.abs(y_true) > 1e-5
    mape = np.mean(np.abs((y_pred[nz] - y_true[nz]) / y_true[nz])) * 100
    da   = np.mean(np.sign(y_pred) == np.sign(y_true)) * 100

    if model_name:
        print(f"{'─'*50}")
        print(f"  {model_name}")
        print(f"  MAE  : {mae:.6f}")
        print(f"  RMSE : {rmse:.6f}")
        print(f"  MAPE : {mape:.2f}%")
        print(f"  Dir. Accuracy: {da:.1f}%  (random baseline = 50%)")

    return {'mae':mae, 'rmse':rmse, 'mape':mape, 'da':da,
            'y_pred':y_pred, 'y_true':y_true}

Multi-step Forecasting

Predicting 5 or 10 steps ahead (a "forecast horizon") is more useful than single-step prediction. Two strategies:

Strategy 1 — Direct Multi-output

Change the output layer from Linear(hidden, 1) to Linear(hidden, H) where H is your forecast horizon. Train with a target of shape (batch, H). Clean and simple.

Strategy 2 — Recursive (Auto-regressive)

Use the single-step model recursively: predict t+1, append it to the input window, predict t+2, etc. Errors compound — each prediction error feeds into the next input. Good for models already trained for single-step.

class LSTMMultiStep(nn.Module):
    """LSTM that predicts H steps ahead in one forward pass."""
    def __init__(self, input_size, hidden_size=128, num_layers=2,
                 dropout=0.3, horizon=5):
        super().__init__()
        self.lstm    = nn.LSTM(input_size, hidden_size, num_layers,
                              batch_first=True, dropout=dropout)
        self.norm    = nn.LayerNorm(hidden_size)
        self.dropout = nn.Dropout(dropout)
        # Output: horizon values instead of 1
        self.fc = nn.Linear(hidden_size, horizon)

    def forward(self, x):
        out, _ = self.lstm(x)
        last   = self.norm(out[:, -1, :])
        return self.fc(self.dropout(last))  # (batch, horizon)

# Build sequences with H-step targets
def make_sequences_multistep(X, window, target_col, horizon):
    xs, ys = [], []
    for i in range(len(X) - window - horizon + 1):
        xs.append(X[i : i + window])
        # Target: the next `horizon` values of the target column
        ys.append(X[i + window : i + window + horizon, target_col])
    return np.array(xs, dtype=np.float32), np.array(ys, dtype=np.float32)

def recursive_forecast(model, seed_window, n_steps):
    """
    Given a seed window (shape: seq_len × features), autoregressively
    predict the next n_steps values.
    
    Warning: this only updates the TARGET COLUMN in the sliding window.
    The other features are held constant from the last observed step
    (a reasonable simplification for short horizons).
    """
    model.eval()
    window = seed_window.copy()   # don't modify original
    forecasts = []

    with torch.no_grad():
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            x_t = torch.tensor(window[np.newaxis], dtype=torch.float32).to(DEVICE)
            pred = model(x_t).cpu().item()
            forecasts.append(pred)
            # Slide window: drop oldest, append new row
            new_row = window[-1].copy()           # copy last feature row
            new_row[target_idx] = pred             # overwrite with predicted return
            window = np.vstack([window[1:], new_row])

    return np.array(forecasts)

            dropout     = 0.0,   # disable dropout at inference!
        )
        model.load_state_dict(torch.load(f"{checkpoint_prefix}_weights.pt",
                                         map_location='cpu'))
        scaler = joblib.load(f"{checkpoint_prefix}_scaler.pkl")
        print(f"✓ Loaded {config['model_class']} from {checkpoint_prefix}")
        return cls(model, scaler, config)

    def _fetch_and_preprocess(self, end_date=None):
        """Download recent data and apply the same pipeline as training."""
        # Need window + 30 extra days for rolling feature computation
        import datetime
        end  = end_date or datetime.date.today().strftime("%Y-%m-%d")
        # Calculate start: window + 30 buffer days + weekends (×1.5)
        days_needed = int((self.window + 30) * 1.5)
        start = (datetime.datetime.strptime(end, "%Y-%m-%d")
                 - datetime.timedelta(days=days_needed)).strftime("%Y-%m-%d")

        raw = {}
        for t in self.tickers:
            df  = yf.download(t, start=start, end=end, progress=False)
            raw[t] = df["Close"].rename(t)

        prices  = pd.concat(raw.values(), axis=1)
        prices.columns = self.tickers
        prices  = prices.ffill().dropna()

        features = build_features(prices)[self.feat_cols]
        return features

    def predict_next(self, end_date=None):
        """
        Predict the NEXT day's log return for the target asset.
        
        Returns dict with:
          - log_return      : raw model output
          - expected_change : percentage price change
          - direction       : 'UP' or 'DOWN'
          - confidence      : how far prediction is from zero (proxy)
        """
        features = self._fetch_and_preprocess(end_date)

        if len(features) < self.window:
            raise ValueError(f"Need ≥{self.window} rows, got {len(features)}")

        # Scale using saved scaler (NOT a new fit)
        X_scaled = self.scaler.transform(features.values)

        # Take last `window` rows
        window_data = X_scaled[-self.window:].astype(np.float32)
        x_tensor    = torch.tensor(window_data[np.newaxis])  # (1, W, F)

        with torch.no_grad():
            log_ret = self.model(x_tensor).item()

        pct_change = (np.exp(log_ret) - 1) * 100
        return {
            'target'         : self.target_col.replace('_ret', ''),
            'log_return'     : round(log_ret, 6),
            'expected_change': f"{pct_change:+.3f}%",
            'direction'      : 'UP' if log_ret > 0 else 'DOWN',
            'confidence'     : round(abs(log_ret) / 0.02, 3),  # 0.02 ≈ typical daily σ
            'as_of'          : features.index[-1].strftime("%Y-%m-%d"),
        }

Production Checklist

Things to implement before deploying a time series model in any real context:

Data & Pipeline

  • Walk-forward validation — instead of a single train/test split, retrain monthly on an expanding window and evaluate on the next month. Much more realistic than a static split.
  • Feature stability monitoring — log the distribution of input features. Alert if the live distribution drifts significantly from training (concept drift).
  • Handle corporate events — stock splits, dividends, delistings. yfinance usually adjusts automatically, but verify.
  • Missing data handling — build explicit checks: if >3 consecutive days are missing, don't predict (model was not trained on such gaps).

Model

  • Ensemble multiple models — average predictions from GRU + TCN + LSTM. Reduces variance significantly.
  • Confidence intervals — use Monte Carlo Dropout at inference (keep dropout ON, run 100 forward passes, compute std of predictions) to quantify uncertainty.
  • Periodic retraining — market regimes change. Retrain quarterly at minimum. Log all retrain events with their date and test metrics.

Operational

  • Version your models — save each checkpoint with a timestamp and commit hash. You need to be able to reproduce any prediction.
  • Log every prediction — store (date, input_hash, prediction, actual) in a database. This is your ground truth for ongoing evaluation.
  • Never trade on a single model's output — combine with fundamental analysis, position sizing, and risk limits.

def mc_dropout_predict(model, x_tensor, n_samples=100):
    """
    Enable dropout at inference to get uncertainty estimates.
    Returns: mean prediction, std (uncertainty), 95% CI
    """
    model.train()   # keep dropout active
    preds = []
    with torch.no_grad():
        for _ in range(n_samples):
            p = model(x_tensor).item()
            preds.append(p)
    model.eval()    # restore eval mode

    preds  = np.array(preds)
    mean   = preds.mean()
    std    = preds.std()
    ci_low, ci_high = np.percentile(preds, [2.5, 97.5])

    print(f"Prediction (MC Dropout, n={n_samples}):")
    print(f"  Mean log-return : {mean:.6f}  ({(np.exp(mean)-1)*100:+.3f}%)")
    print(f"  Uncertainty (σ) : {std:.6f}")
    print(f"  95% CI          : [{(np.exp(ci_low)-1)*100:+.3f}%,  {(np.exp(ci_high)-1)*100:+.3f}%]")
    return mean, std, (ci_low, ci_high)
